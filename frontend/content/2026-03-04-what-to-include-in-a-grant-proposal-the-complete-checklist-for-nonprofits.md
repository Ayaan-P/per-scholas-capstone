# What to Include in a Grant Proposal: The Complete Checklist for Nonprofits

Writing a grant proposal can feel overwhelming, especially when you're juggling limited staff and countless deadlines. But here's the good news: most funders want the same core elements. Once you understand what to include in a grant proposal—and why each piece matters—you can build templates that save hours on every application.

This guide breaks down every essential component of a winning grant proposal, with practical tips specifically for nonprofit organizations. Whether you're pursuing your first foundation grant or your fiftieth federal application, you'll find everything you need to submit a complete, compelling proposal.

## Quick Overview: The Essential Grant Proposal Components

Before we dive deep, here's what most grant proposals require:

1. **Cover letter** — Your first impression
2. **Executive summary/abstract** — The proposal in miniature
3. **Statement of need** — Why this matters
4. **Project description** — What you'll do and how
5. **Goals and objectives** — Measurable outcomes
6. **Methods and timeline** — Your implementation plan
7. **Evaluation plan** — How you'll measure success
8. **Organizational background** — Why you're qualified
9. **Budget and budget narrative** — The numbers with context
10. **Sustainability plan** — Life after the grant
11. **Appendices** — Supporting documentation

Let's explore each component in detail.

## 1. Cover Letter: Your First Impression

The cover letter is often the first thing a program officer reads. While it's technically optional for some grants, including a polished cover letter demonstrates professionalism and provides a personalized introduction to your proposal.

### What to Include in Your Cover Letter

**Opening paragraph:**
- Name of your organization and its mission
- The specific grant program you're applying for
- The amount you're requesting
- A one-sentence summary of your project

**Middle paragraph(s):**
- Brief explanation of the problem you're addressing
- Why your organization is uniquely positioned to solve it
- How the project aligns with the funder's priorities

**Closing paragraph:**
- Your appreciation for the funder's consideration
- Contact information for follow-up questions
- An authorized signature

### Cover Letter Best Practices

**Keep it to one page.** Program officers review dozens of proposals. A concise cover letter shows respect for their time.

**Address it to a specific person.** "Dear Program Officer" is acceptable, but "Dear Ms. Johnson" is better. A quick phone call to the foundation can often yield the reviewer's name.

**Mirror the funder's language.** If they prioritize "community resilience," use that phrase—not a synonym. This signals you've done your homework.

**Lead with impact, not your needs.** Instead of "We need funding to..." try "This project will serve 500 families in..." Funders invest in outcomes, not organizational survival.

## 2. Executive Summary or Abstract

The executive summary is the most important section of your proposal. Many reviewers read only this section before deciding whether the full proposal deserves their attention. For federal grants, the abstract often appears in public databases, so it also serves as your project's public face.

### What Your Executive Summary Must Include

- **Organization name and location** — Who you are and where you work
- **Project title** — Clear and descriptive
- **Total budget and amount requested** — The numbers upfront
- **Problem statement** — What issue you're addressing (2-3 sentences)
- **Target population** — Who will benefit and how many
- **Project description** — What you'll actually do (3-4 sentences)
- **Expected outcomes** — Specific, measurable results
- **Timeline** — Project duration

### Writing Tips for Your Executive Summary

**Write it last.** Even though it appears first, your executive summary should be written after completing the full proposal. You'll have a clearer picture of your project and stronger language to draw from.

**Keep it under 250 words** for most foundation grants. Federal grants typically specify a word or character limit—follow it exactly.

**Make every sentence work.** There's no room for filler. If a sentence doesn't add critical information, cut it.

**Avoid jargon.** Your abstract may be read by board members outside your field. Use plain language that anyone can understand.

**Include one compelling statistic.** Numbers catch attention. "42% of children in our district lack access to afterschool programming" is more memorable than "many children lack access."

## 3. Statement of Need: Making the Case

The statement of need (also called the problem statement or needs assessment) establishes why your project matters. This section answers a fundamental question: Why should the funder care?

A strong statement of need accomplishes three things:
1. Describes a significant problem using credible data
2. Connects the problem to the funder's priorities
3. Sets up your organization's solution as the logical response

### Crafting a Compelling Statement of Need

**Lead with the community, not your organization.** The problem is not that your nonprofit lacks resources—it's that families in your community lack food security, students lack mentorship, or seniors lack transportation. Frame the need around the people you serve.

**Use credible, current data.** Funders expect evidence. The best sources include:
- Census data and American Community Survey statistics
- State and county health department reports
- Academic research and peer-reviewed studies
- Local needs assessments and community surveys
- Reports from national organizations in your field

**Cite your sources.** Every statistic should have a source. This builds credibility and shows you've done rigorous research.

**Make it local.** National statistics provide context, but funders want to know about your specific community. After citing a national trend, zoom in: "While 15% of American children experience food insecurity, in Jefferson County that number rises to 23%."

**Explain why the problem persists.** What barriers have prevented solutions? Understanding root causes demonstrates sophisticated thinking and prepares the reader for your proposed approach.

**Connect to the funder's mission.** If the foundation prioritizes educational equity, frame your afterschool program in terms of closing opportunity gaps. Every statement of need should implicitly answer: "Why is this funder the right partner for this work?"

### Statement of Need: Example Structure

1. **Hook** — One compelling sentence or statistic that captures attention
2. **Context** — Background on the issue at national/regional level
3. **Local focus** — Data specific to your community
4. **Affected population** — Who suffers and how
5. **Barriers** — Why existing solutions haven't worked
6. **Gap your project fills** — What's missing that your project provides
7. **Urgency** — Why now?

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Circular reasoning:** "We need funding because we lack funding" isn't a statement of need—it's a statement of want.
- **Outdated data:** Using statistics from 2015 in a 2026 proposal undermines credibility.
- **Exaggeration:** Inflated claims make reviewers skeptical of your entire proposal.
- **Doom and gloom:** Paint a serious picture, but don't suggest the problem is unsolvable. Funders invest in hope.

## 4. Project Description: Your Solution in Action

The project description is the heart of your proposal. This section explains exactly what you plan to do, how you'll do it, and who will benefit. Reviewers should finish this section understanding your project so clearly they could explain it to someone else.

### Key Elements of a Strong Project Description

**Theory of change:** Explain the logic connecting your activities to your outcomes. If you provide job training, how does that lead to employment? If you offer counseling, how does that reduce substance abuse? Make the connections explicit.

**Target population:** Describe exactly who will participate in your program. How many people? What demographics? How will you recruit them? What eligibility criteria apply?

**Program activities:** Detail what will actually happen. If you're running workshops, how often? How long? What curriculum? Who facilitates? The more concrete, the more credible.

**Staffing plan:** Who will implement the project? What are their qualifications? Include brief bios of key personnel. If you're hiring new staff, describe the positions and required qualifications.

**Partners and collaborations:** List any organizations you'll work with and their roles. Include letters of commitment in your appendices.

**Geographic scope:** Where will activities take place? Be specific about locations, service areas, and any expansion plans.

### Writing Your Project Description

**Use active, confident language.** Write "We will provide" not "We hope to provide" or "We plan to try to provide."

**Be specific, not vague.** Instead of "regular meetings," write "bi-weekly 90-minute sessions." Instead of "ongoing support," write "monthly check-in calls for 12 months post-program."

**Address potential challenges.** Acknowledge what could go wrong and how you'll handle it. This demonstrates realistic planning, not pessimism.

**Show innovation—or explain proven methods.** Either your approach is new and promising, or it's evidence-based and proven. Make clear which it is and why that matters.

## 5. Goals and Objectives: What Success Looks Like

Goals and objectives define what your project will achieve. While often used interchangeably, they're distinct:

- **Goals** are broad, long-term aspirations (e.g., "Reduce food insecurity in Jefferson County")
- **Objectives** are specific, measurable steps toward those goals (e.g., "Distribute 10,000 meals to 500 families over 12 months")

### Writing SMART Objectives

Every objective should be SMART:

- **Specific:** Clearly defines what will be accomplished
- **Measurable:** Includes a quantifiable target
- **Achievable:** Realistic given your resources
- **Relevant:** Connected to your goals and the funder's priorities
- **Time-bound:** Includes a deadline

**Weak objective:** "Improve participants' job readiness"
**SMART objective:** "75% of program participants will complete the 6-week job readiness curriculum and obtain employment within 90 days of program completion"

### Process vs. Outcome Objectives

**Process objectives** describe activities (outputs):
- "Conduct 24 financial literacy workshops serving 200 individuals"
- "Train 50 volunteers in trauma-informed care"

**Outcome objectives** describe changes (impact):
- "60% of participants will increase their credit scores by 50+ points"
- "Participants will report a 40% reduction in anxiety symptoms"

Most proposals need both types. Process objectives show you'll complete activities; outcome objectives show those activities matter.

### How Many Objectives?

Three to five objectives is typically appropriate. Too few suggests an underdeveloped project; too many becomes overwhelming and hard to evaluate. Each objective should connect directly to your evaluation plan.

## 6. Methods and Timeline: Your Implementation Plan

This section details how and when you'll accomplish your objectives. Reviewers want to see that you've thought through the practical realities of implementation.

### Methods Section Content

**Activities in sequence:** Walk through your project from start to finish. What happens first? What follows? How do activities build on each other?

**Staffing responsibilities:** Who is responsible for each activity? Be specific about roles.

**Participant flow:** How do people enter your program? Progress through it? Exit successfully?

**Materials and resources:** What will you need? Curriculum, equipment, space, supplies?

### Creating Your Timeline

A timeline (sometimes called a work plan) shows activities mapped against time. Options include:

**Simple table format:**
| Activity | Month 1-3 | Month 4-6 | Month 7-9 | Month 10-12 |
|----------|-----------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Staff hiring | X | | | |
| Training | X | X | | |
| Service delivery | | X | X | X |
| Evaluation | | | | X |

**Gantt chart:** Visual bar chart showing activity duration—effective but requires more formatting.

**Narrative timeline:** Month-by-month description—appropriate when activities are complex or don't fit a table.

### Timeline Tips

**Build in startup time.** Hiring, training, and setup take longer than expected. A realistic timeline accounts for the ramp-up period.

**Show milestones.** Mark key achievements: "First cohort enrolled," "Mid-project evaluation complete," "Final report submitted."

**Account for grant requirements.** Many funders require interim reports at specific points. Build those deadlines into your timeline.

**Be realistic about capacity.** If you're a small nonprofit, don't propose activities that would require your staff to work 80-hour weeks.

## 7. Evaluation Plan: Proving Your Impact

Funders don't just want to fund good work—they want to fund work that demonstrably works. Your evaluation plan explains how you'll measure success and learn from your efforts.

### Components of an Evaluation Plan

**Evaluation questions:** What do you want to know? These typically align with your objectives.
- "Did participants complete the program?"
- "Did participants' skills improve?"
- "Did participants obtain employment?"

**Indicators:** What specific measures will tell you if you've succeeded?
- Completion rates
- Pre/post test score changes
- Employment verification

**Data collection methods:** How will you gather information?
- Surveys and questionnaires
- Pre/post assessments
- Attendance records
- Interviews and focus groups
- Administrative data (employment records, academic records)

**Data collection timeline:** When will you collect data? At intake, monthly, at completion, 6 months post-program?

**Analysis plan:** How will you make sense of the data? Basic descriptive statistics? Comparison to baseline? Qualitative coding?

**Who conducts evaluation:** Internal staff? External evaluator? Combination?

### Process vs. Outcome Evaluation

**Process evaluation** examines implementation:
- Are we reaching our target population?
- Are participants attending as expected?
- Are we delivering services as planned?

**Outcome evaluation** examines results:
- Did participants' situations improve?
- Were objectives achieved?
- What impact did the program have?

Strong evaluation plans include both. Process evaluation helps you improve; outcome evaluation proves you succeeded.

### Evaluation Plan Tips

**Match rigor to stakes.** A $10,000 foundation grant doesn't need a randomized controlled trial. A multi-year federal grant might.

**Plan for learning, not just reporting.** Good evaluation helps you improve your program, not just satisfy funders.

**Be honest about limitations.** You can't prove causation without a control group. Acknowledge what your evaluation design can and cannot demonstrate.

**Budget for evaluation.** Data collection and analysis require time and often money. Include evaluation costs in your budget (typically 5-15% of project costs for external evaluation).

## 8. Organizational Background: Why You're the Right Partner

This section establishes your nonprofit's credibility and capacity. Funders want to know you can actually deliver what you're proposing.

### What to Include

**Mission and history:** What your organization exists to do and how long you've been doing it.

**Relevant experience:** Previous projects similar to what you're proposing. Results you've achieved. Awards or recognition.

**Organizational capacity:** Staff qualifications, board expertise, financial stability, infrastructure.

**Geographic knowledge:** Your understanding of and relationships within the community you serve.

**Track record with grants:** Successful grant management history. Positive relationships with other funders.

### Positioning Your Organization

**If you're established:** Emphasize your proven track record, organizational stability, and lessons learned from previous work.

**If you're newer:** Emphasize staff expertise, board strength, strategic partnerships, and the fresh perspective you bring.

**If this is new territory:** Explain how your existing strengths transfer to this new area. Highlight partnerships with experienced organizations.

## 9. Budget and Budget Narrative: Making the Numbers Make Sense

The budget tells funders exactly how you'll spend their money. The budget narrative explains why each cost is necessary. Together, they demonstrate that you've planned carefully and will use resources responsibly.

### Budget Categories

**Personnel:**
- Salaries and wages for staff working on the project
- Fringe benefits (health insurance, retirement, taxes)
- Calculate as percentage of time devoted to the project

**Consultants and contractors:**
- External experts, trainers, evaluators
- Include rates and estimated hours

**Supplies and materials:**
- Office supplies, program materials, curriculum
- Equipment under the capitalization threshold (usually $5,000)

**Equipment:**
- Major purchases over the capitalization threshold
- May require justification and multiple bids

**Travel:**
- Staff travel for program activities
- Conference attendance
- Mileage reimbursement

**Other direct costs:**
- Printing and copying
- Communications (phone, internet)
- Participant costs (incentives, stipends, childcare)
- Rent or space costs directly attributable to the project

**Indirect costs:**
- Overhead expenses (administration, facilities, utilities)
- Usually calculated as a percentage of direct costs
- Federal grants often have negotiated indirect cost rates

### Writing Your Budget Narrative

The budget narrative (sometimes called budget justification) explains each line item. For each cost, explain:
- What it is
- Why it's necessary for the project
- How you calculated the amount

**Example:**
"Project Coordinator (1.0 FTE) — $52,000. The Project Coordinator manages day-to-day implementation, supervises program staff, coordinates partner activities, and ensures timely reporting. Salary based on market rate for comparable positions in our region."

### Budget Tips

**Make the math work.** Triple-check your arithmetic. A budget that doesn't add up signals sloppiness.

**Be realistic, not minimal.** Underfunding your project sets you up for failure. Request what you actually need.

**Include matching funds.** If you have other funding sources, show them. This demonstrates organizational capacity and reduces the funder's risk.

**Follow funder guidelines.** Some costs are ineligible (alcohol, lobbying, certain overhead). Read the guidelines carefully.

**Align with your narrative.** If your project description mentions home visits, your budget should include mileage. If you mention training, include trainer costs.

## 10. Sustainability Plan: Life After the Grant

Funders want to invest in lasting change, not temporary relief. Your sustainability plan explains how the project's impact will continue after grant funding ends.

### Sustainability Strategies

**Diversified funding:**
- Other grants you'll pursue
- Individual donors you'll cultivate
- Fee-for-service revenue
- Earned income opportunities

**Cost reduction:**
- Volunteer integration
- Donated goods and services
- Efficiency improvements after initial implementation

**Institutionalization:**
- Integration into your organization's core operations
- Adoption by partner organizations
- Policy changes that sustain the work

**Community ownership:**
- Training community members to continue activities
- Building local leadership
- Creating structures that persist beyond your organization

### Writing Your Sustainability Plan

**Be specific.** "We will seek other funding" is vague. "We have submitted applications to the XYZ Foundation and are cultivating a major donor with capacity for multi-year support" is concrete.

**Be realistic.** Not every project continues indefinitely, and that's okay. Some projects complete their purpose. If yours is time-limited, explain why that's appropriate.

**Show a track record.** If you've sustained previous projects, mention it. This demonstrates you understand sustainability isn't just an afterthought.

## 11. Appendices: Supporting Documentation

Appendices provide evidence and context without cluttering your main proposal. Include only what's requested or genuinely useful.

### Common Appendix Items

**Organizational documents:**
- IRS 501(c)(3) determination letter
- List of board members and affiliations
- Organizational budget for current fiscal year
- Annual report or audited financial statements

**Project support:**
- Letters of commitment from partners
- Memoranda of understanding
- Resumes of key staff
- Job descriptions for positions to be hired

**Evidence and context:**
- Needs assessment data
- Logic model or theory of change diagram
- Relevant research citations
- Maps showing service area

**Program materials:**
- Sample curriculum
- Evaluation instruments (surveys, assessments)
- Marketing materials

### Appendix Tips

**Only include what's requested or essential.** Extra materials don't earn bonus points and suggest you don't follow directions.

**Reference appendices in your narrative.** "See Appendix C for letters of support" tells reviewers the documentation exists.

**Make sure everything is current.** Outdated board lists or expired MOU's raise red flags.

## Putting It All Together: Your Proposal Checklist

Before you submit, verify your proposal includes:

- [ ] Cover letter (if applicable)
- [ ] Executive summary/abstract
- [ ] Statement of need with cited data
- [ ] Project description with clear activities
- [ ] SMART goals and objectives
- [ ] Detailed methods and timeline
- [ ] Evaluation plan with specific measures
- [ ] Organizational background
- [ ] Complete, accurate budget
- [ ] Budget narrative justifying all costs
- [ ] Sustainability plan
- [ ] All required appendices
- [ ] Correct formatting (font, margins, page limits)
- [ ] All signatures and certifications

## Final Tips for Grant Proposal Success

**Follow directions exactly.** If the RFP says 12-point Times New Roman, use 12-point Times New Roman. If it says 10-page limit, stay under 10 pages. Program officers notice when applicants can't follow basic instructions.

**Write for tired reviewers.** Use clear headings, short paragraphs, and bullet points for key information. Make your proposal easy to skim.

**Tell a coherent story.** Your need, goals, methods, and evaluation should connect logically. A reader should see exactly how each piece supports the others.

**Get feedback before submitting.** Ask a colleague unfamiliar with your project to read your draft. If they're confused, reviewers will be too.

**Submit early.** Online systems crash. Attachments fail to upload. Give yourself a buffer before the deadline.

**Keep records.** Save your submitted proposal. You'll want to reference it when writing progress reports—or when applying for continuation funding.

## Start Building Your Grant Proposal Template

Now that you know what to include in a grant proposal, you can build templates for your most common grant types. Create a master document with your organizational background, standard budget categories, and boilerplate language that you customize for each application.

The first proposal takes the longest. After that, you're not starting from scratch—you're adapting and improving.
