*Your comprehensive roadmap to navigating Uniform Guidance, the 12 compliance requirements, and single audit obligations*

Federal grants represent a critical funding source for nonprofits across the United States, with billions of dollars flowing to organizations addressing education, healthcare, housing, environmental protection, and social services. But accessing these funds comes with significant responsibilities. Federal grant compliance isn't just bureaucratic box-checking—it's the framework that ensures taxpayer dollars achieve their intended impact while protecting your organization from audit findings, funding clawbacks, and reputational damage.

The 2026 federal grant landscape has shifted significantly. New executive orders have tightened oversight expectations, the Office of Management and Budget (OMB) has updated the Uniform Guidance with major threshold changes, and agencies are placing greater emphasis on measurable outcomes and digital administration. For nonprofit leaders, understanding these compliance requirements isn't optional—it's essential for organizational sustainability.

This comprehensive guide walks you through everything you need to know about federal grant compliance in 2026, from the foundational 12 compliance requirements to single audit obligations, subrecipient monitoring, and practical strategies for building compliance systems that work.

## What Is Federal Grant Compliance?

Federal grant compliance refers to your organization's adherence to all rules, regulations, and requirements associated with receiving and spending federal funds. These requirements come from multiple sources:

**The Uniform Guidance (2 CFR Part 200)**: The primary regulatory framework governing federal awards to non-federal entities, including nonprofits. This comprehensive guidance covers administrative requirements, cost principles, and audit requirements.

**Program-specific requirements**: Each federal grant program has unique rules outlined in the Notice of Funding Opportunity (NOFO), award terms and conditions, and program compliance supplements.

**Federal awarding agency regulations**: Individual agencies like HHS, HUD, DOE, and EPA may have additional requirements beyond the Uniform Guidance.

**Executive orders and policy directives**: Current administration priorities that shape grant making and compliance expectations.

Compliance isn't a single moment—it's continuous. From the moment you apply for federal funding through post-award closeout and beyond, your organization must maintain systems that ensure every dollar is spent appropriately, documented thoroughly, and reported accurately.

## The 12 Compliance Requirements Under Uniform Guidance

The Uniform Guidance establishes 12 key compliance requirements that auditors test during single audits. Understanding each requirement is fundamental to building effective compliance systems.

### 1. Activities Allowed and Unallowed

Every federal grant specifies which activities you can and cannot use grant funds to support. Allowable activities must directly relate to the grant's objectives and align with the terms of your award agreement.

**What this means in practice:**
- Review your grant agreement and program regulations thoroughly before spending any funds
- Document how each expenditure connects to approved program activities
- When in doubt about whether an activity is allowed, contact your program officer before proceeding
- Keep copies of any written approvals for activities that fall in gray areas

**Common violations:**
- Using grant funds for activities outside the approved scope
- Expanding program activities without prior approval
- Supporting lobbying activities (almost universally prohibited with federal funds)

### 2. Allowable Costs and Cost Principles

Not all expenses are created equal in the eyes of federal funders. Allowable costs must meet four criteria established in 2 CFR 200.403:

**Necessary**: The cost is essential for the performance of the federal award
**Reasonable**: The cost reflects what a prudent person would pay under similar circumstances
**Allocable**: The cost is properly assigned to the federal award based on the benefit received
**Consistently applied**: You treat similar costs the same way across all funding sources

The Uniform Guidance also specifies certain costs that are always unallowable, including:
- Alcoholic beverages
- Entertainment expenses
- Lobbying costs
- Bad debts
- Contingency provisions
- Fundraising costs

**Direct vs. indirect costs**: Direct costs are expenses that can be specifically identified with a particular grant (salaries for program staff, program supplies, travel for grant activities). Indirect costs are expenses that benefit multiple programs and cannot be easily attributed to a single grant (rent, utilities, administrative salaries).

### 3. Cash Management

Federal agencies expect you to minimize the time between receiving federal funds and disbursing them for program purposes. This requirement exists to reduce the government's borrowing costs and ensure efficient use of taxpayer dollars.

**Key principles:**
- Request federal funds as close as possible to when you need to make payments
- If you receive advance payments, disburse those funds promptly
- Maintain procedures to ensure timely payment of grant-related expenses
- Track cash balances by funding source to avoid commingling

**Interest requirements**: If you hold federal funds that earn interest, you may be required to return interest earned over $500 per year to the federal government (requirements vary by program).

### 4. Eligibility

Many federal programs restrict who can receive services or participate in funded activities. Eligibility requirements protect program integrity by ensuring benefits reach the intended populations.

**Your responsibilities:**
- Understand all eligibility criteria for program participants or beneficiaries
- Establish and document procedures for determining eligibility
- Maintain records that demonstrate eligibility verification
- Re-verify eligibility as required by program rules

**Examples of eligibility requirements:**
- Income thresholds for housing assistance programs
- Geographic restrictions for community development grants
- Age requirements for youth programs
- Citizenship or immigration status for certain federal benefits

### 5. Equipment and Real Property Management

Federal grants often fund the purchase of equipment or improvements to real property. When they do, specific rules govern how you acquire, use, track, and ultimately dispose of these assets.

**Equipment** is defined as tangible personal property with a useful life of more than one year and an acquisition cost of $10,000 or more per unit (this threshold increased from $5,000 under the 2024 Uniform Guidance revisions).

**Requirements include:**
- Maintaining property records that include description, serial number, source of funding, acquisition date and cost, percentage of federal participation, location, and condition
- Conducting physical inventories at least every two years
- Implementing a control system to prevent loss, damage, or theft
- Using equipment primarily for the authorized federal purpose
- Following proper disposition procedures when equipment is no longer needed

### 6. Matching, Level of Effort, and Earmarking

Many federal grants require recipients to contribute their own resources to the funded program or maintain certain funding levels.

**Matching requirements** specify that you must provide a certain percentage of program costs from non-federal sources. A 25% match on a $100,000 grant, for example, means you must contribute $25,000 of your own funds.

**Level of effort requirements** prevent you from using federal funds to replace (supplant) existing funding. You must maintain your prior level of spending on the activity.

**Earmarking requirements** specify that certain percentages of grant funds must be spent on particular activities, populations, or geographic areas.

**Documentation is critical:**
- Track and document all matching contributions
- Ensure matching funds meet federal criteria (typically cannot include other federal funds)
- Maintain records demonstrating continued level of effort
- Monitor spending against earmarking requirements throughout the grant period

### 7. Period of Performance

Federal grants have specific start and end dates—the period of performance. Generally, you can only charge costs incurred during this period to the grant.

**Critical rules:**
- Costs incurred before the start date or after the end date are typically unallowable
- You must liquidate all financial obligations within 120 calendar days after the period of performance ends
- No-cost extensions may be available but often require advance approval
- Some pre-award costs may be allowable if specifically authorized

**Best practices:**
- Set internal deadlines well before the official end date
- Monitor encumbrances and open purchase orders as the grant winds down
- Plan for closeout requirements throughout the grant, not just at the end
- Request extensions early if you anticipate needing additional time

### 8. Procurement and Suspension and Debarment

When you use federal funds to purchase goods or services, you must follow specific procurement standards designed to ensure open competition and prevent waste.

**General procurement requirements:**
- Maintain written procurement policies and procedures
- Conduct all procurement in a manner providing full and open competition
- Document the rationale for vendor selection
- Avoid conflicts of interest in procurement decisions

**Procurement methods** vary based on the size of the purchase:
- **Micro-purchases** (up to $10,000): May be made without competitive quotes
- **Small purchases** ($10,000 to $250,000): Require price or rate quotes from an adequate number of qualified sources
- **Competitive proposals/sealed bids** (over $250,000): Formal competitive processes required

**Suspension and debarment**: Before making covered transactions (typically over $25,000), you must verify that vendors are not excluded from participating in federal programs. Check the System for Award Management (SAM.gov) exclusions database.

### 9. Program Income

Program income is revenue generated by grant-supported activities. This includes fees for services, sale of commodities, usage or rental fees, and other income directly resulting from the grant.

**How program income is treated** depends on your award terms:
- **Deduction method**: Program income is deducted from total allowable costs (reducing your federal share)
- **Addition method**: Program income is added to committed federal funds (increasing your total budget)
- **Cost sharing method**: Program income counts toward your matching requirement

**Requirements:**
- Account for program income throughout the grant period
- Use program income for allowable costs of the program that generated it
- Report program income as required by the awarding agency
- Maintain records that identify the source and application of program income

### 10. Reporting

Accurate and timely reporting is fundamental to federal grant compliance. You'll typically submit several types of reports throughout the grant period.

**Financial reports** document how you've spent grant funds, typically using the Federal Financial Report (SF-425). Frequency varies by program—quarterly, semi-annually, or annually.

**Performance reports** describe progress toward achieving grant objectives, outcomes achieved, and any challenges encountered. These demonstrate that your spending is producing results.

**Special reports** may be required for specific events (significant developments, project changes) or by certain programs.

**Critical reporting practices:**
- Know all reporting deadlines and build them into your calendar
- Establish internal deadlines that allow time for review before submission
- Ensure financial and performance reports are consistent with each other
- Maintain documentation supporting all reported data
- Submit reports on time—late reporting can trigger payment holds or award termination

### 11. Subrecipient Monitoring

If your organization passes federal funds to other organizations (subrecipients), you become responsible for their compliance. This "pass-through" responsibility is one of the most complex and commonly problematic compliance areas.

**Before issuing subawards:**
- Determine whether the relationship is truly a subrecipient arrangement (versus a contractor/vendor relationship)
- Conduct a risk assessment of each subrecipient
- Include all required terms and conditions in subaward agreements
- Communicate relevant federal requirements to subrecipients

**Ongoing monitoring responsibilities:**
- Review financial and performance reports from subrecipients
- Conduct on-site monitoring visits based on risk
- Follow up on findings from subrecipient audits
- Provide training and technical assistance as needed
- Maintain documentation of all monitoring activities

**When subrecipients fail**: You're responsible for ensuring compliance. If a subrecipient has issues, you must take corrective action—up to and including terminating the subaward and potentially recovering funds.

### 12. Special Tests and Provisions

Some federal programs have unique compliance requirements beyond the 11 areas above. These "special tests and provisions" are program-specific and outlined in the OMB Compliance Supplement.

**Examples:**
- HUD programs may have specific environmental review requirements
- Education programs may have specific maintenance of effort calculations
- Health programs may have specific patient confidentiality requirements

**Your approach:**
- Review the Compliance Supplement for each program you administer
- Identify any special tests and provisions that apply
- Build systems to ensure compliance with program-specific requirements
- Document compliance with these unique requirements

## The Single Audit: What Nonprofits Need to Know in 2026

The single audit, also known as the OMB A-133 audit, is a comprehensive examination of your organization's federal award programs and compliance with federal requirements.

### Who Requires a Single Audit?

As of fiscal years beginning on or after October 1, 2024, nonprofits that expend **$1 million or more** in federal awards during their fiscal year must undergo a single audit. This threshold increased from the previous $750,000 level that had been in place since 1997.

**Important nuance for 2025-2026**: The new threshold applies to awards made on or after October 1, 2024. Many organizations will have awards under both the old and new thresholds simultaneously. You must track when each award was made to determine which threshold applies.

### What the Single Audit Covers

The single audit has two main components:

**Financial statement audit**: An examination of your organization's overall financial statements, including an opinion on whether they present fairly your financial position.

**Compliance audit**: Testing of your compliance with federal requirements for major programs. Auditors select major programs based on a risk-based approach and federal expenditure thresholds.

Auditors will test your compliance with the 12 compliance requirements for each major program, examining controls and performing detailed tests of transactions.

### Preparing for Your Single Audit

Start preparing for your single audit throughout the year, not just at year-end:

**Maintain organized documentation**: Every transaction charged to federal grants should be supported by documentation demonstrating the cost is allowable, properly approved, and correctly coded.

**Reconcile regularly**: Monthly reconciliations between your general ledger and grant tracking systems help identify issues early.

**Complete the Schedule of Expenditures of Federal Awards (SEFA)**: This schedule lists all federal awards expended during the year. Maintain supporting documentation for every amount reported.

**Address prior audit findings**: If you had findings in previous years, demonstrate corrective action and document that issues have been resolved.

**Prepare your audit team**: Ensure staff who will work with auditors understand the programs, can answer questions, and know where documentation is located.

### Understanding Audit Findings

Single audit findings are categorized by severity:

**Material weaknesses**: Significant deficiencies in internal control that create a reasonable possibility of material noncompliance going undetected. These are serious and require immediate attention.

**Significant deficiencies**: Less severe than material weaknesses but still important enough to merit attention.

**Questioned costs**: Costs that auditors believe may not comply with federal requirements. You may be required to return questioned costs if the awarding agency determines they are unallowable.

All findings require a corrective action plan. Persistent findings can affect your risk assessment for future audits and may impact your ability to receive additional federal funding.

## Indirect Costs: Understanding the 2026 Landscape

Indirect costs—the overhead expenses that support federal programs but can't be attributed to a single grant—remain one of the most complex and sometimes contentious aspects of federal grant management.

### The De Minimis Rate Increase to 15%

The 2024 Uniform Guidance revisions increased the de minimis indirect cost rate from 10% to 15% of modified total direct costs. This change, effective October 1, 2024, benefits organizations that don't have a negotiated indirect cost rate agreement (NICRA).

**What this means for your organization:**
- If you've never had a NICRA, you can claim up to 15% of modified total direct costs as indirect costs
- The de minimis rate is available without submitting a cost allocation plan
- Once you elect the de minimis rate, you must use it consistently until you negotiate a rate
- This is a floor, not a ceiling—organizations with higher actual indirect costs should consider negotiating a NICRA

### Negotiated Indirect Cost Rate Agreements

Organizations with significant federal funding often benefit from negotiating an indirect cost rate with their cognizant federal agency. A NICRA may allow you to recover a higher percentage of indirect costs than the de minimis rate.

**The negotiation process:**
- Identify your cognizant agency (typically the agency providing the most federal funding)
- Prepare an indirect cost rate proposal documenting your actual indirect costs
- Submit your proposal and supporting documentation
- Work with the agency to negotiate an approved rate

**Types of NICRAs:**
- **Provisional rate**: An estimated rate used during the current period, subject to adjustment later
- **Final rate**: Established after actual costs are known, representing the final indirect cost rate for a completed period
- **Predetermined rate**: An agreed-upon rate for a current or future period that is not subject to adjustment
- **Fixed rate with carry-forward**: A rate that includes an adjustment for over- or under-recovery from prior periods

### Current Tensions Around Indirect Costs

The 2026 landscape includes ongoing debates about indirect cost rates. Some federal agencies and policymakers have pushed for caps on indirect cost rates, arguing that high rates divert funds from direct services. Nonprofits and their advocates counter that indirect costs represent real expenses necessary to deliver programs effectively.

Executive Order 14332, "Improving Oversight of Federal Grantmaking," issued in August 2025, has increased scrutiny of grant administration costs. While the full implications are still unfolding, nonprofits should:
- Document the actual costs supporting their indirect cost rates
- Be prepared to justify indirect costs to funders
- Monitor policy developments that could affect indirect cost recovery

## Building Compliance Systems That Work

Effective federal grant compliance isn't about heroic efforts at audit time—it's about systems and procedures that make compliance the natural result of daily operations.

### Develop Comprehensive Written Policies

Your organization needs written policies addressing every aspect of federal grant management:

**Financial policies:**
- Chart of accounts and coding procedures for federal awards
- Cost allocation methodology
- Cash management procedures
- Equipment management and inventory procedures
- Procurement policies meeting federal standards
- Record retention requirements

**Program policies:**
- Eligibility determination procedures
- Subrecipient selection and monitoring procedures
- Performance measurement and reporting procedures
- Procedures for requesting budget modifications or no-cost extensions

**Review and update** these policies regularly—at least annually and whenever regulations change significantly.

### Implement Strong Internal Controls

Internal controls are the procedures that prevent, detect, and correct compliance problems:

**Segregation of duties**: No single person should control all aspects of a financial transaction. Separate the functions of authorization, custody of assets, and recordkeeping.

**Authorization levels**: Establish clear authority levels for approving expenditures, signing contracts, and making program decisions.

**Documentation requirements**: Define what documentation is required for different types of transactions and ensure staff understand these requirements.

**Review and approval processes**: Build review checkpoints into your processes—transaction approvals, supervisor reviews, reconciliation procedures.

**Monitoring**: Regularly review compliance through internal audits, management reviews, or other monitoring activities.

### Train Your Team

Compliance is everyone's responsibility. Invest in training for:

**Program staff**: Understanding allowable activities, eligibility requirements, and documentation expectations

**Finance staff**: Proper coding, allowable costs, cost allocation, and financial reporting requirements

**Leadership**: Overall compliance responsibilities, audit requirements, and corrective action processes

**New employees**: Orientation to federal grant requirements relevant to their roles

Consider annual refresher training on Uniform Guidance requirements and any regulatory changes.

### Leverage Technology

Grant management software can significantly enhance compliance:

- Track awards, budgets, and expenditures by funding source
- Generate reports aligned with federal requirements
- Flag potential compliance issues before they become findings
- Maintain documentation in an organized, searchable format
- Facilitate subrecipient monitoring

Choose systems appropriate for your organization's size and complexity. Even small organizations can benefit from using accounting software with robust grant tracking features.

### Create a Compliance Calendar

With multiple grants, multiple deadlines, and multiple compliance requirements, a compliance calendar is essential:

- Report due dates for all federal awards
- Single audit timeline and deadlines
- Equipment inventory dates
- Policy review dates
- Training schedule
- Registration renewal dates (SAM.gov, Grants.gov, etc.)
- Grant end dates and closeout deadlines

Build in internal deadlines that allow time for review before external due dates.

## Navigating the 2026 Federal Grant Environment

The 2026 federal grant landscape presents both challenges and opportunities for nonprofits. Understanding current trends helps you position your organization for success.

### Increased Oversight and Accountability

Executive Order 14332 and related policy changes reflect heightened focus on grant oversight. Federal agencies are:
- Strengthening risk assessments of grant applicants
- Increasing monitoring of award recipients
- Emphasizing measurable outcomes and performance accountability
- Reviewing and potentially reducing grant programs

**Your response:**
- Invest in strong compliance infrastructure before you need it
- Document your impact with robust outcome data
- Be proactive in communicating with program officers
- Prepare for more rigorous application reviews and monitoring

### Digital-First Administration

Federal grant administration continues shifting toward digital platforms:
- Grants.gov for application submission
- SAM.gov for entity registration and exclusions checking
- eRA Commons, Grants Online, and other agency-specific systems
- Electronic reporting and documentation

**Your response:**
- Ensure all registrations are current and accurate
- Designate staff responsible for maintaining system access
- Build digital documentation practices into your workflow
- Stay informed about system changes and updates

### New Certification Requirements

Recent policy proposals would require new certifications from federal grant recipients related to DEI practices, immigration enforcement cooperation, and other areas. While these requirements are still evolving, they signal:
- More detailed certification requirements for grant recipients
- Potential for increased legal complexity
- Need for careful review of award terms and conditions

**Your response:**
- Review all certifications carefully before signing
- Consult with legal counsel on new or unclear requirements
- Document your organization's position on compliance with certified requirements
- Monitor policy developments that could affect your eligibility or compliance obligations

### Budget Uncertainty

The federal budget environment remains volatile, with proposed cuts to many programs that fund nonprofit services:
- NIH facing significant proposed reductions
- HUD programs under budget pressure
- EPA and NSF facing potential cuts

**Your response:**
- Diversify your funding portfolio
- Build scenario plans for reduced federal funding
- Strengthen relationships with non-federal funders
- Demonstrate your impact to make the case for continued funding

## Common Compliance Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Learning from others' mistakes can save your organization from costly compliance failures:

### Insufficient Documentation

**The problem:** Transactions that can't be adequately supported with documentation may be disallowed as questioned costs.

**The solution:** Establish clear documentation requirements for every type of transaction. Train staff to document as they go, not after the fact. Conduct periodic reviews to ensure documentation standards are being met.

### Poor Time and Effort Tracking

**The problem:** Personnel costs are often the largest grant expense, and inadequate time tracking leads to questioned costs and audit findings.

**The solution:** Implement a time tracking system that captures actual time worked on each grant. Ensure time records are prepared contemporaneously (not reconstructed later), reflect actual work performed, and are properly approved.

### Procurement Violations

**The problem:** Failing to follow competitive procurement requirements is one of the most common findings in single audits.

**The solution:** Know your thresholds and follow appropriate procurement procedures for each purchase level. Document the procurement process, including how you ensured competition and why you selected the winning vendor.

### Missing Cost Allocation

**The problem:** Charging costs to grants that should be allocated across multiple funding sources—or failing to charge grants for their fair share of shared costs.

**The solution:** Develop a written cost allocation plan. Apply allocation methodologies consistently. Review cost allocation at least quarterly to ensure it reflects actual benefit.

### Weak Subrecipient Monitoring

**The problem:** Passing federal funds to other organizations without adequate oversight can result in compliance failures that affect your organization.

**The solution:** Conduct thorough risk assessments before making subawards. Build monitoring requirements into subaward agreements. Follow through with actual monitoring activities—don't just have policies on paper.

### Late Reporting

**The problem:** Missing reporting deadlines can trigger payment holds, affect your risk assessment, and damage your relationship with funders.

**The solution:** Build a reporting calendar with internal deadlines that allow time for review. Assign clear responsibility for each report. Have backup plans if the responsible person is unavailable.

## Building a Culture of Compliance

Ultimately, federal grant compliance depends on organizational culture as much as systems and procedures. Compliance must be:

**Valued by leadership**: Board and executive commitment to ethical grant management sets the tone for the organization.

**Resourced appropriately**: Compliance takes time and sometimes money. Underfunding compliance functions creates risk.

**Integrated into operations**: Compliance shouldn't be an afterthought or someone else's job. It's built into how everyone does their work.

**Continuously improved**: Learn from audit findings, near-misses, and changes in the regulatory environment. Compliance systems should evolve.

## Conclusion: Compliance as a Competitive Advantage

Federal grant compliance requirements may seem burdensome, but they serve important purposes: ensuring accountability for public funds, creating level playing fields for grant competition, and building confidence that grant dollars achieve their intended impact.

For nonprofits that master compliance, these requirements become a competitive advantage. Strong compliance systems:
- Reduce audit findings and questioned costs
- Build funder confidence in your organization
- Position you well for larger and more complex awards
- Protect your organization's reputation and sustainability
- Free staff to focus on mission rather than crisis management

The 2026 federal grant environment demands excellence in compliance. By understanding the 12 compliance requirements, preparing for single audits, building robust systems, and staying current with regulatory changes, your nonprofit can access federal funding confidently while fulfilling your stewardship responsibilities.

Start where you are. Assess your current compliance systems. Identify gaps. Build improvements incrementally. And remember: compliance isn't about perfection—it's about continuous progress toward strong, sustainable grant management practices.
