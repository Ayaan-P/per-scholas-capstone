# How to Write a Letter of Inquiry (LOI): The Complete Guide for Nonprofits

A letter of inquiry (LOI) is often your first—and sometimes only—chance to capture a foundation's attention. Get it right, and you'll receive an invitation to submit a full proposal. Get it wrong, and your organization never gets a second look.

This guide covers everything you need to know about writing letters of inquiry that open doors to foundation funding.

## What Is a Letter of Inquiry?

A letter of inquiry (also called a letter of intent or LOI) is a brief, focused document that introduces your organization and project to a potential funder. Think of it as a proposal abstract—a concise pitch that helps foundations quickly assess whether your request aligns with their priorities.

### Why Foundations Use LOIs

- **Efficiency**: Program officers review hundreds of requests; LOIs help them screen quickly
- **Alignment check**: Foundations can assess fit before investing time in full proposals
- **Relationship building**: LOIs open dialogue before the formal application process
- **Resource management**: Both sides avoid wasted effort on mismatched requests

### LOI vs. Full Proposal

| Element | Letter of Inquiry | Full Proposal |
|---------|------------------|---------------|
| Length | 1-3 pages | 10-25+ pages |
| Purpose | Interest screening | Detailed request |
| Budget | Summary only | Line-item detail |
| Timeline | Overview | Detailed milestones |
| Attachments | Usually minimal | Extensive documentation |

## When to Submit an LOI

### Foundations That Require LOIs

Many foundations mandate LOIs before accepting full proposals. Check the funder's guidelines for:
- "Letter of inquiry required"
- "Do not submit unsolicited proposals"
- "LOI process" or "preliminary application"

### Strategic LOI Opportunities

Even when not required, an LOI can be strategic:
- **New funder relationships**: Introducing your organization
- **Testing alignment**: Before investing in a full proposal
- **Timing questions**: When you're unsure about deadlines or priorities

## The Anatomy of an Effective LOI

### 1. Opening Paragraph (The Hook)

Your first paragraph must accomplish three things:
1. State who you are
2. State what you're requesting
3. Connect to the funder's priorities

**Strong opening example:**
> "The Riverside Youth Alliance seeks a $50,000 grant from the Johnson Family Foundation to expand our after-school STEM program to three additional low-income neighborhoods in East Hartford. This request aligns with your foundation's commitment to educational equity and closing the opportunity gap for underserved students."

**Weak opening example:**
> "The Riverside Youth Alliance is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit organization founded in 2018 that serves youth in the Hartford area through various programs."

The first version gets to the point. The second buries the request.

### 2. Organization Description (Your Credibility)

In 2-3 sentences, establish:
- Your mission
- Your track record
- Why you're positioned to succeed

**Example:**
> "Since 2018, Riverside Youth Alliance has served over 2,500 students through after-school programming in Hartford's underserved communities. Our STEM curriculum, developed in partnership with local engineers and teachers, has demonstrated measurable improvements in science proficiency and career aspirations. Last year, 94% of participating students maintained grade-level performance in math and science."

### 3. Problem Statement (The Need)

Clearly articulate:
- The specific problem you're addressing
- Who is affected
- What happens if the problem isn't addressed

Use data, but keep it digestible:
> "In East Hartford's low-income neighborhoods, only 23% of students have access to after-school STEM programming, compared to 78% in affluent districts. This disparity correlates with lower science proficiency scores and reduced interest in STEM careers. Our program addresses this gap directly."

### 4. Project Description (Your Solution)

Explain:
- What you will do
- How you will do it
- What makes your approach effective

**Be specific but concise:**
> "We will establish three new after-school STEM sites, each serving 40 students (grades 4-8) with 15 hours of weekly programming. Our curriculum emphasizes hands-on projects—from robotics to environmental science—taught by credentialed teachers and volunteer engineers. Students progress through skill levels, culminating in a community science fair."

### 5. Goals and Outcomes (Measurable Impact)

State what success looks like:
- Quantitative outcomes (numbers served, scores improved)
- Qualitative outcomes (skills gained, opportunities created)
- Timeline for achieving results

**Example:**
> "By the end of Year 1, we will:
> - Enroll 120 students across three sites
> - Achieve 90% regular attendance
> - Demonstrate measurable improvement in science confidence scores
> - Connect 80% of students with STEM career mentors"

### 6. Budget Summary

Provide high-level figures:
- Total project cost
- Amount requested
- Other funding sources (confirmed and pending)

**Example:**
> "Total project cost: $125,000
> Request to Johnson Family Foundation: $50,000 (40%)
> Confirmed funding: $45,000 (Hartford Community Foundation, corporate sponsors)
> Pending: $30,000 (applications in progress)"

### 7. Closing (The Ask)

End with:
- A clear ask
- Offer to provide additional information
- Contact details

**Example:**
> "We respectfully request the opportunity to submit a full proposal for the Johnson Family Foundation's consideration. I am available to discuss this initiative at your convenience and can provide additional information as needed. Thank you for your commitment to educational equity in our community."

## LOI Template

```
[Your Organization Letterhead]

[Date]

[Program Officer Name]
[Foundation Name]
[Address]

Dear [Program Officer Name or "Foundation Team"],

[Opening paragraph: Organization name, amount requested, project purpose, connection to funder priorities]

[Organization description: Mission, track record, credibility - 2-3 sentences]

[Problem statement: The need you're addressing, with supporting data - 1 paragraph]

[Project description: What you'll do, how you'll do it - 1-2 paragraphs]

[Goals and outcomes: Measurable results - bullet points work well]

[Budget summary: Total cost, amount requested, other funding]

[Closing: Clear ask, offer to provide more information, gratitude]

Sincerely,

[Your name]
[Title]
[Organization]
[Contact information]
```

## Common LOI Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

### 1. Burying the Ask

**Problem**: Many LOIs spend the first paragraph on organizational history before getting to the request.

**Fix**: Lead with your request. Program officers want to know immediately: How much? For what?

### 2. Generic Language

**Problem**: Using boilerplate language that could apply to any funder.

**Fix**: Reference specific funder priorities and explain the connection to your project.

### 3. Too Much Detail

**Problem**: Treating the LOI like a condensed full proposal.

**Fix**: Stay high-level. The LOI should generate interest, not answer every question.

### 4. Weak Problem Statement

**Problem**: Describing what you want to do without establishing why it matters.

**Fix**: Ground your request in documented need with data and context.

### 5. Vague Outcomes

**Problem**: Promising "impact" without defining what that means.

**Fix**: State specific, measurable outcomes with timelines.

### 6. Ignoring Guidelines

**Problem**: Submitting LOIs that don't follow the foundation's requirements.

**Fix**: Read guidelines carefully. If they ask for two pages, don't send three.

### 7. Missing the Human Element

**Problem**: Focusing only on data and programs, not people.

**Fix**: Include brief stories or examples that illustrate the human impact.

## What Happens After You Submit

### Typical Outcomes

1. **Invitation to submit full proposal** — Success! Follow up promptly.
2. **Request for more information** — A good sign; respond thoroughly.
3. **Decline with feedback** — Learn from it; ask if you can reapply.
4. **Decline without feedback** — May indicate misalignment; research before reapplying.
5. **No response** — Follow up appropriately (see below).

### Follow-Up Protocol

- **Wait time**: 4-6 weeks before following up (unless guidelines specify)
- **Method**: Email is usually appropriate; phone only if invited
- **Tone**: Professional, not pushy; express continued interest

**Sample follow-up:**
> "Dear [Name], I wanted to follow up on the letter of inquiry we submitted on [date] regarding [project name]. We remain enthusiastic about this opportunity and would be happy to provide any additional information. Please let me know if there are questions I can answer."

## Advanced LOI Strategies

### Research Before Writing

Before drafting your LOI:
- Review the foundation's recent grants
- Read program officer interviews or speeches
- Study their annual reports
- Look for connections (board members, previous grantees)

### Customize for Each Funder

Never send identical LOIs to multiple foundations. Customize:
- The opening connection to their priorities
- The language you use (formal vs. conversational)
- The outcomes you emphasize (what matters most to them?)

### Build Relationships First

When possible, connect before the LOI:
- Attend funder briefings or webinars
- Introduce yourself at conferences
- Seek introductions from mutual contacts

### Time Your Submission

- **Check deadlines**: Some foundations have cycles; others accept rolling submissions
- **Avoid fiscal year-end rush**: December submissions often face more competition
- **Allow response time**: Submit early enough to complete the full proposal process

## Quick Checklist: Before You Submit

- [ ] Request amount is clearly stated in the first paragraph
- [ ] Organization credibility is established briefly
- [ ] Problem is documented with data
- [ ] Solution is specific and actionable
- [ ] Outcomes are measurable
- [ ] Budget summary is included
- [ ] Length follows guidelines
- [ ] Tone matches the foundation's style
- [ ] Contact information is complete
- [ ] Proofread by someone outside the project

## Conclusion

A letter of inquiry is your opening move in the foundation funding relationship. Done well, it demonstrates your organization's professionalism, clarity of purpose, and alignment with funder priorities. Done poorly, it closes doors before you even get to knock.

Invest the time to craft an LOI that's concise, compelling, and customized to each funder. Your full proposal may be where you make the detailed case—but the LOI is where you earn the right to make it.
