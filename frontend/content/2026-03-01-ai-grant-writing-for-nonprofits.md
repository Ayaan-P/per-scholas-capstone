## What AI Can (and Can't) Do for Grant Writing

Before diving into tools and tactics, let's set realistic expectations. AI grant writing technology has advanced significantly, but it's not magic—it's leverage.

### What AI Does Well

**Research and discovery.** AI excels at synthesizing large amounts of information quickly. It can help you research potential funders, analyze their giving patterns, summarize lengthy RFPs, and identify alignment between your programs and funder priorities.

**First-draft generation.** Given proper context about your organization and the specific grant opportunity, AI can generate rough drafts of proposal sections in minutes rather than hours. These drafts require editing, but they eliminate the "blank page" problem that slows many writers down.

**Boilerplate and background sections.** The organizational history, mission statement, and program descriptions that appear in nearly every proposal are perfect candidates for AI assistance. Once you've trained an AI tool on your organization's voice and key facts, it can generate these sections with minimal oversight.

**Data analysis and visualization.** Need to summarize program outcomes, create charts for your appendix, or identify trends in your impact data? AI tools can analyze datasets and generate visualizations faster than any human could.

**Editing and refinement.** AI is surprisingly effective at tightening prose, checking for consistency, adjusting tone for different funders, and catching errors that human reviewers miss.

### Where AI Falls Short

**Strategic judgment.** AI can't decide which grants are worth pursuing, how to prioritize limited staff time, or whether a particular funder is actually a good fit for your organization's trajectory. These decisions require human context and strategic thinking.

**Emotional resonance.** The best grant proposals tell stories that move reviewers. AI can structure narratives and suggest language, but the authentic emotional core—the lived experience of your beneficiaries, the passion of your staff—must come from humans.

**Funder-specific nuance.** Every funder has subtle preferences, unwritten rules, and relationship dynamics that AI can't fully grasp. A foundation program officer you've built a relationship with over years expects a different proposal than a first-time federal application.

**Ethical judgment.** AI can inadvertently introduce biases, make claims that aren't quite accurate, or suggest approaches that don't align with your values. Human oversight isn't optional—it's essential.

**Understanding your actual work.** AI doesn't know what happened at last week's community meeting or why your executive director is passionate about this specific program expansion. It can only work with the context you provide.

The bottom line: AI is an efficiency multiplier, not a replacement for grant writing expertise. Used well, it can help a skilled grant writer produce twice as much in half the time. Used poorly, it produces generic proposals that funders immediately recognize and reject.

## Building Your AI Grant Writing Workflow

Adopting AI isn't just about choosing tools—it's about redesigning your process to take advantage of what AI does well while preserving human judgment where it matters.

### Step 1: Start with Grant Matching (Before Writing Anything)

The most sophisticated AI writing tool in the world can't help you if you're pursuing the wrong grants. Before any writing begins, you need to identify opportunities that genuinely align with your mission, capacity, and strategic priorities.

This is where grant matching platforms like **FundFish** come in. Rather than spending hours manually searching databases or relying on word-of-mouth about funding opportunities, AI-powered grant matching can surface relevant opportunities based on your organization's specific programs, geography, and funding needs.

The efficiency gains from writing are meaningless if you're writing proposals for grants you're unlikely to win. Start with better matching, then apply AI writing tools to the opportunities that make strategic sense.

### Step 2: Gather and Organize Source Material

AI generates better outputs when you feed it better inputs. Before starting any proposal, compile:

- **Organizational boilerplate**: Mission statement, history, leadership bios, annual budget, accreditations
- **Program-specific content**: Program descriptions, theory of change, logic models, evaluation frameworks
- **Impact data**: Outcomes from recent years, success stories, testimonials, demographic data
- **The RFP itself**: Guidelines, eligibility requirements, evaluation criteria, submission format
- **Funder research**: Previous grants they've made, their stated priorities, their language and terminology

Many AI grant writing tools let you upload these documents directly, creating an organizational "brain" that informs all subsequent outputs.

### Step 3: Use AI for First Drafts, Not Final Drafts

The most effective AI workflow treats AI outputs as raw material, not finished products.

**Generate section by section.** Rather than asking AI to write an entire proposal, break the work into specific sections: executive summary, statement of need, project description, evaluation plan, organizational capacity, budget narrative. Tackle each one individually with specific prompts.

**Provide detailed context.** Don't just say "write a statement of need." Tell the AI about the specific community you serve, the data points that illustrate the problem, the unique positioning of your organization, and the funder's known priorities. The more context, the better the output.

**Iterate aggressively.** Treat the first AI output as a starting point. Ask for revisions, provide feedback, and refine until the draft captures what you actually want to say.

### Step 4: Add Human Voice and Judgment

Once you have AI-generated drafts, the real work begins: infusing them with authentic voice, strategic positioning, and emotional resonance.

**Read everything out loud.** AI prose often sounds smooth but generic. Reading aloud reveals awkward phrasing, missing personality, and sections that don't quite ring true.

**Add specific stories and examples.** AI can suggest narrative structures, but the actual stories—the beneficiary whose life changed, the volunteer who went above and beyond, the unexpected obstacle your team overcame—must come from your lived experience.

**Check every fact.** AI can confidently state things that aren't true. Verify statistics, dates, dollar amounts, and any claims about your organization or the funder.

**Ensure strategic alignment.** Does this proposal advance your organization's actual priorities? Does it set realistic expectations you can actually deliver on? AI doesn't know your strategic plan.

### Step 5: Review and Quality Control

Before submission, every AI-assisted proposal needs rigorous human review:

- **Accuracy check**: Are all facts, figures, and claims correct?
- **Tone check**: Does this sound like your organization, or like a robot?
- **Funder alignment**: Have you addressed their specific priorities and evaluation criteria?
- **Feasibility check**: Are you promising anything you can't deliver?
- **Compliance check**: Have you followed all formatting requirements, page limits, and submission guidelines?

Some organizations create formal review checklists. Others assign different reviewers for different aspects. Whatever your approach, don't skip this step—AI-generated content that goes out unreviewed will eventually embarrass you.

## Best Practices for AI Grant Writing

Drawing from what's working for nonprofits across the sector, here are the practices that separate successful AI adoption from disappointing experiments.

### Master the Art of Prompting

The quality of AI output depends enormously on the quality of your prompts. Vague instructions produce vague results.

**Bad prompt:** "Write a grant proposal for our youth program."

**Good prompt:** "Write a 500-word project description for our after-school STEM program for middle schoolers in Oakland. The funder (Acme Foundation) prioritizes measurable outcomes, college readiness, and programs serving low-income communities. Our program has served 150 students annually since 2019, with 85% showing improved math scores. Include our partnership with Oakland Unified School District and our 1:6 mentor-to-student ratio."

The more specific context you provide, the more useful the output.

### Create Organizational Templates

Don't start from scratch with every proposal. Develop standardized content that can be adapted:

- Organizational background (2-3 versions at different lengths)
- Program descriptions for each major initiative
- Leadership bios
- Diversity, equity, and inclusion statement
- Financial stability narrative
- Evaluation approach boilerplate

These become the building blocks that AI assembles and adapts for specific opportunities.

### Maintain a "Brain" of Previous Proposals

Many AI tools allow you to upload past proposals and organizational documents. Take advantage of this—your previous winning proposals contain language, structure, and positioning that works. AI can learn from and build upon this foundation.

Update this repository regularly with new program data, outcomes, and success stories.

### Develop Clear Workflows and Accountability

AI changes who does what in the grant writing process. Clarify:

- Who provides source material?
- Who prompts and iterates with AI?
- Who reviews and edits outputs?
- Who gives final approval?
- Who submits?

Document these workflows so the process doesn't depend on tribal knowledge.

### Know When AI Isn't the Answer

Not every proposal benefits from AI assistance. Consider skipping AI for:

- **Very small, simple grants** where drafting manually might be faster than setting up prompts
- **Highly relationship-dependent funders** who expect a personal touch they'll notice is missing
- **First-time applications to major funders** where you want maximum strategic control
- **Extremely technical proposals** that require deep subject-matter expertise AI doesn't have

AI is a tool. Use it when it helps; don't force it when it doesn't.

## Risks and Ethical Considerations

Adopting AI for grant writing isn't without risks. Responsible use requires awareness of potential pitfalls.

### Accuracy and Hallucinations

AI systems can generate confident, plausible-sounding content that is simply wrong. They may invent statistics, misattribute quotes, or make claims about your organization that aren't true.

**Mitigation:** Verify every factual claim. Never submit AI-generated content without human review.

### Privacy and Data Security

When you input organizational data, beneficiary information, or funder research into AI tools, where does that data go? Some systems use inputs to train their models, potentially exposing sensitive information.

**Mitigation:** Read privacy policies carefully. Avoid inputting personally identifiable information about beneficiaries. Consider tools that guarantee data privacy and don't train on user inputs.

### Bias and Representation

AI systems reflect biases present in their training data. This can result in content that unintentionally reinforces stereotypes, excludes perspectives, or misrepresents communities.

**Mitigation:** Review outputs critically for bias. Have diverse reviewers examine content. Be especially careful with language describing the communities you serve.

### Over-Reliance and Skill Atrophy

If AI does all the writing, your team's grant writing skills may deteriorate. This creates vulnerability if AI tools change, become unavailable, or simply don't work for certain opportunities.

**Mitigation:** Use AI to augment human capability, not replace it. Maintain core grant writing skills through training and practice.

### Funder Backlash

Some funders have begun noticing (and complaining about) the flood of AI-generated proposals that sound polished but lack authenticity. Proposals that feel generic or "too smooth" may be penalized.

**Mitigation:** Always infuse AI-generated content with authentic voice, specific examples, and genuine organizational personality. If it sounds like it could have come from any nonprofit, rewrite it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is it ethical to use AI for grant writing?

Yes, when used responsibly. AI is a tool, like spellcheck or a word processor. The ethical considerations are about transparency (don't claim AI-generated content is entirely hand-written if asked), accuracy (verify everything), and authenticity (ensure the final product genuinely represents your organization). Most funders don't prohibit AI assistance, though this may evolve.

### Will funders know if I used AI?

Probably not, if you've done the work to humanize the content. Funders increasingly recognize that AI is part of modern workflows. What they can detect—and dislike—is generic, personality-free proposals that could have come from anyone. The goal isn't to hide AI use; it's to produce genuinely excellent proposals regardless of what tools helped create them.

### How much time does AI actually save?

Organizations using purpose-built tools report completing proposals in one-third to one-half of the usual time. General-purpose tools save less because they require more prompting and oversight. The biggest time savings come from boilerplate generation, first-draft creation, and research synthesis.

### Should I tell my board we're using AI for grants?

Transparency is generally wise. Frame it accurately: AI is a productivity tool that helps staff work more efficiently, not a replacement for human judgment and expertise. If your board has concerns, develop an AI usage policy that addresses data privacy, quality control, and oversight.

### What if a funder prohibits AI?

Some funders, particularly in academic and government contexts, have begun specifying limitations on AI use. Read guidelines carefully and comply. When in doubt, ask the program officer directly.

### Can AI help with grant reporting too?

Absolutely. Many of the same tools that help with proposals can assist with progress reports, final reports, and renewal applications. AI is particularly useful for synthesizing program data and generating narrative summaries of outcomes.

### How do I choose between different AI tools?

Consider your budget, grant volume, team size, and technical comfort. Small nonprofits with limited budgets might start with ChatGPT and Grantboost's free tier. Larger organizations submitting complex federal grants might invest in Grant Assistant or similar comprehensive platforms. Most organizations end up using a combination of tools for different purposes.
