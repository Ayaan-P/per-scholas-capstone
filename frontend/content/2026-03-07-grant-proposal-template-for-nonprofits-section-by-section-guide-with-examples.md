# Grant Proposal Template for Nonprofits: Section-by-Section Guide with Examples

Writing a grant proposal from scratch is one of the most intimidating tasks a nonprofit faces. You're staring at a blank page, knowing that thousands of dollars—sometimes hundreds of thousands—depend on what you write next.

But here's a secret that experienced grant writers know: most successful proposals follow the same fundamental structure. The funders who read hundreds of proposals actually *want* predictability. They're looking for specific information in specific places, and the proposals that make their job easy are the ones that win.

This template breaks down each section of a standard grant proposal, shows you exactly what to include, and provides fill-in frameworks you can adapt to your organization. We've also included examples from successful proposals across different cause areas so you can see these principles in action.

**What this guide covers:**
- The 10 essential sections of a grant proposal
- Fill-in templates for each section
- Real examples from funded proposals
- Common mistakes and how to avoid them
- Adapting templates for different funder types

## Understanding Grant Proposal Structure

Before diving into the template, it's important to understand why proposals are structured the way they are.

Funders—whether private foundations, government agencies, or corporate giving programs—are making investment decisions. They need to answer three fundamental questions:

1. **Is there a real problem worth solving?** (Statement of Need)
2. **Can this organization solve it?** (Organizational Capacity + Track Record)
3. **Will my money be used effectively?** (Budget + Evaluation Plan)

Every section of your proposal should help answer one of these questions. If a paragraph doesn't serve one of these purposes, cut it.

### When to Use a Template vs. Follow Funder Guidelines

Some funders provide detailed application guidelines with specific questions and page limits. In those cases, follow their instructions exactly—the template becomes a reference for what to include in each answer.

Other funders, especially smaller family foundations, simply ask for "a proposal" with minimal guidance. That's when a standard template is invaluable.

## Section 1: Cover Letter

**Purpose:** Make a strong first impression, summarize your request, and establish relevance to the funder's priorities.

**Length:** 1 page maximum

### Template

```
[Your Organization Letterhead]

[Date]

[Program Officer Name]
[Foundation Name]
[Address]

Dear [Mr./Ms./Mx. Last Name],

[Opening: Connect your mission to the funder's priorities in one sentence.]

[Organization Name] respectfully requests a grant of $[amount] to support [specific project or program]. This [timeframe] initiative will [primary outcome] for [number] [beneficiaries] in [geographic area].

[One paragraph: Brief organizational description—founding date, mission, current scope of work.]

[One paragraph: Why this project now? What's the opportunity or urgency?]

[One paragraph: Why this funder? Reference specific alignment with their stated priorities.]

The attached proposal provides full details on our approach, timeline, and budget. We would welcome the opportunity to discuss this request further. Please contact [Name] at [phone] or [email] with any questions.

Sincerely,

[Signature]
[Executive Director Name]
[Title]
```

### Example: Youth Education Nonprofit

> Dear Ms. Rodriguez,
>
> Bright Futures Academy respectfully requests a grant of $75,000 from the Martinez Family Foundation to expand our after-school STEM program to three additional Title I schools in Phoenix. This two-year initiative will provide hands-on science and coding education to 450 middle school students from low-income families.
>
> Founded in 2015, Bright Futures Academy has served over 2,000 students through our evidence-based after-school programming. Our students show an average 34% improvement in science assessment scores and 89% report increased interest in STEM careers.
>
> The pandemic widened the STEM opportunity gap for low-income students who lacked access to quality enrichment programs. Our expansion addresses this gap at a critical moment when schools are rebuilding engagement.
>
> The Martinez Family Foundation's commitment to educational equity in Arizona makes this partnership especially meaningful. Your previous investments in after-school programming demonstrate a shared belief that quality enrichment should be available to all students regardless of family income.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Generic openings** like "We are writing to request..." — lead with impact or connection instead
- **Focusing on your organization's needs** ("We need funding to...") rather than community impact
- **Missing the personal connection** — address a specific person, not "Dear Foundation"
- **Exceeding one page** — if you can't summarize it briefly, the full proposal needs work

## Section 2: Executive Summary

**Purpose:** Provide a complete overview that could stand alone. Many program officers read this first to decide whether to continue.

**Length:** 1-2 pages

### Template

```
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

[Organization Name] requests $[amount] from [Funder Name] to [primary activity] for [beneficiary population] in [location].

**The Need**
[2-3 sentences describing the problem with at least one statistic.]

**Our Solution**
[2-3 sentences describing your approach and why it works.]

**Expected Outcomes**
- [Quantified outcome 1]
- [Quantified outcome 2]
- [Quantified outcome 3]

**Organizational Capacity**
[2-3 sentences on relevant experience, track record, or unique qualifications.]

**Budget Summary**
Total Project Cost: $[amount]
Requested from [Funder]: $[amount]
Secured from Other Sources: $[amount]
Pending: $[amount]

**Timeline**
[Start date] to [End date]
```

### Example: Food Security Organization

> **EXECUTIVE SUMMARY**
>
> Harvest Hope Food Bank requests $50,000 from the Regional Community Foundation to establish three mobile food distribution sites serving rural communities in Chesterfield and Lancaster counties.
>
> **The Need**
> Rural food insecurity in our region has increased 47% since 2019, yet existing food pantries are concentrated in urban areas. Residents in food deserts travel an average of 23 miles to reach the nearest food assistance provider—a barrier that leaves 3,200 families without consistent access to nutrition assistance.
>
> **Our Solution**
> Our mobile pantry program brings fresh produce, proteins, and shelf-stable items directly to underserved communities through a refrigerated truck serving predetermined locations on a weekly schedule. This model has proven effective in three pilot communities, where we reduced food insecurity rates by 31% within the first year of operation.
>
> **Expected Outcomes**
> - Serve 400 households monthly across three new distribution sites
> - Distribute 180,000 pounds of nutritious food annually
> - Reduce average distance to food assistance from 23 miles to under 5 miles
> - Connect 200 families with SNAP enrollment assistance
>
> **Organizational Capacity**
> Harvest Hope has distributed 8.4 million pounds of food annually since 1982. Our existing mobile pantry fleet serves 12 communities, and we maintain partnerships with 47 local farms for fresh produce sourcing.
>
> **Budget Summary**
> Total Project Cost: $127,500
> Requested from Regional Community Foundation: $50,000
> Secured from Corporate Sponsors: $52,500
> Pending from State Agriculture Department: $25,000
>
> **Timeline**
> July 2026 through June 2028 (24 months)

### Expert Tips

- **Write this section last** even though it appears first—you'll summarize more effectively after completing the full proposal
- **Front-load the most important information** in each paragraph; assume readers may skim
- **Include at least three quantified outcomes** to demonstrate measurability
- **Match the budget summary to your detailed budget** exactly—discrepancies raise red flags

## Section 3: Statement of Need

**Purpose:** Convince the funder that the problem is real, urgent, and solvable. This is where you build the case for why funding is necessary.

**Length:** 1-2 pages

### Template

```
STATEMENT OF NEED

**The Problem**
[Opening paragraph: Hook the reader with a specific story, statistic, or observation that illustrates the problem. Make it human.]

**By the Numbers**
[2-3 paragraphs with statistics from credible sources—census data, research studies, local assessments. Each statistic should support your case for urgency.]

**Root Causes**
[1-2 paragraphs explaining why this problem exists. Show that you understand the systemic issues, not just symptoms.]

**Why Now**
[1 paragraph on urgency—what's changed recently? What opportunity exists? Why is this the right moment for intervention?]

**Community Voice**
[Quote or insight from community members, clients, or partners that validates the need. Shows you've listened to those affected.]

**The Gap**
[1 paragraph explaining what's missing—why existing services or programs haven't solved this problem.]
```

### Key Elements That Make Statements of Need Compelling

**Localize your data.** National statistics provide context, but local data makes the case. "Childhood poverty affects 11.6 million children nationally" is less compelling than "1,847 children in Franklin County live below the poverty line—a 23% increase since 2020."

**Show trends, not just snapshots.** Funders want to know if a problem is growing, stable, or improving. "Food insecurity increased 47% since 2019" is stronger than "12% of residents are food insecure."

**Address the "why" not just the "what."** Funders want to invest in solutions that address root causes. If you're addressing youth unemployment, explain *why* young people in your community struggle to find jobs—skills gaps, transportation barriers, discrimination, lack of networks.

**Include community voice.** A quote from someone affected by the problem adds authenticity. "The food bank saved us when my husband lost his job during COVID. Without it, I don't know how we would have fed our kids." — Maria, program participant.

### Credible Data Sources

- **Census Bureau / American Community Survey** — demographics, income, housing
- **County Health Rankings** — health indicators by county
- **Feeding America Map the Meal Gap** — food insecurity by county
- **Bureau of Labor Statistics** — employment data
- **Local school district data** — free/reduced lunch rates, graduation rates
- **United Way ALICE reports** — Asset Limited, Income Constrained, Employed households
- **Your own program data** — waitlists, unmet demand, client demographics

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Making it about your organization** — this section is about the community, not you
- **Using only national statistics** — localize everything
- **Presenting the problem as hopeless** — funders want to invest in solutions
- **Forgetting to explain why existing solutions aren't working** — acknowledge the landscape

## Section 4: Organizational Background

**Purpose:** Establish credibility. Show that you have the track record, expertise, and stability to deliver on your promises.

**Length:** 1-2 pages

### Template

```
ORGANIZATIONAL BACKGROUND

**Who We Are**
[Organization Name] is a [501(c)(3) nonprofit / other status] founded in [year] to [original mission]. Today, we [current mission and primary activities] serving [annual reach/beneficiaries] in [geographic area].

**Our Approach**
[1-2 paragraphs describing your theory of change or methodology. What makes your approach distinctive or effective?]

**Track Record**
[Highlight 3-5 relevant accomplishments with quantified results where possible.]
- [Accomplishment 1 with numbers]
- [Accomplishment 2 with numbers]
- [Accomplishment 3 with numbers]

**Financial Stability**
[Brief statement on annual budget, revenue diversification, and any relevant financial health indicators.]

Annual Operating Budget: $[amount]
Revenue Sources: [e.g., 45% government grants, 30% foundation grants, 15% individual donations, 10% earned income]

**Leadership and Staff**
[Brief description of leadership team's relevant experience. Include board composition if notable.]

**Partnerships**
[List key partners and their roles in your work, especially those relevant to the proposed project.]
```

### What Funders Look For

**Relevant experience.** Have you done this type of work before? If this is a new program area, how does your existing expertise transfer?

**Demonstrated results.** Outcomes from past programs, especially if similar to what you're proposing.

**Financial health.** Signs of stability—diverse revenue, reasonable reserves, clean audits.

**Qualified leadership.** Staff and board with relevant expertise.

**Community connections.** Partnerships, coalitions, and roots in the community you serve.

### Example: Environmental Nonprofit

> **Who We Are**
> Green Rivers Conservation, a 501(c)(3) nonprofit founded in 1998, protects and restores waterways in the Piedmont region through advocacy, education, and hands-on restoration projects. We engage 3,000 volunteers annually and have protected over 47,000 acres of riparian habitat.
>
> **Our Approach**
> We combine scientific monitoring with community engagement, believing that lasting conservation requires local stewardship. Our "Adopt-a-Stream" model trains community groups to monitor water quality and conduct ongoing maintenance, creating sustained impact beyond initial restoration.
>
> **Track Record**
> - Restored 23 miles of streambank habitat since 2015, reducing sediment pollution by 34%
> - Trained 450 volunteer water quality monitors who collected 12,000 data points in 2025
> - Successfully advocated for three local erosion control ordinances
> - Engaged 85 schools in watershed education reaching 7,500 students annually
>
> **Financial Stability**
> Annual Operating Budget: $890,000
> Revenue Sources: 35% government grants, 30% foundation grants, 20% individual donations, 10% corporate sponsors, 5% earned income
>
> We maintain a 4-month operating reserve and have received clean audits for 15 consecutive years.
>
> **Leadership**
> Executive Director Sarah Chen, MS Environmental Science, brings 18 years of watershed management experience. Our 12-member board includes environmental scientists, business leaders, and community representatives from each county we serve.

## Section 5: Project Description

**Purpose:** Explain exactly what you'll do, how you'll do it, and who will be involved. This is the heart of your proposal.

**Length:** 2-4 pages

### Template

```
PROJECT DESCRIPTION

**Project Overview**
[Program Name] will [primary activity] for [beneficiary population] in [location] over [timeframe]. This project addresses [need] by [approach].

**Goals and Objectives**

Goal 1: [Broad outcome you're working toward]
  - Objective 1.1: [Specific, measurable outcome with target number and timeline]
  - Objective 1.2: [Specific, measurable outcome with target number and timeline]

Goal 2: [Broad outcome you're working toward]
  - Objective 2.1: [Specific, measurable outcome with target number and timeline]
  - Objective 2.2: [Specific, measurable outcome with target number and timeline]

**Target Population**
[Who will be served? How were they identified? What are their characteristics?]

**Methods and Activities**
[Detailed description of what you will actually do. Break into phases or key activities.]

Phase 1: [Name] ([Timeline])
- [Activity 1]
- [Activity 2]

Phase 2: [Name] ([Timeline])
- [Activity 1]
- [Activity 2]

**Staffing**
[Who will implement the project? What are their qualifications?]

**Partners**
[What organizations are involved and what roles do they play?]
```

### Writing Effective Goals and Objectives

**Goals** are broad statements of desired change:
- "Reduce youth unemployment in District 7"
- "Improve health outcomes for seniors living alone"

**Objectives** are specific, measurable steps toward goals. Use SMART criteria:

- **S**pecific: Exactly what will happen?
- **M**easurable: How will you know it happened?
- **A**chievable: Is this realistic?
- **R**elevant: Does this contribute to the goal?
- **T**ime-bound: By when?

**Weak objective:** "Provide job training to young people"

**SMART objective:** "By June 2027, 150 young adults ages 18-24 will complete our 8-week workforce readiness program, with 70% securing employment within 90 days of completion."

### Example: Healthcare Access Program

> **Goals and Objectives**
>
> **Goal 1: Increase access to preventive healthcare for uninsured adults**
>
> - Objective 1.1: By December 2027, operate 24 free health screening events in underserved neighborhoods, serving at least 1,800 individuals
> - Objective 1.2: Connect 80% of participants with abnormal findings to follow-up care within 30 days
> - Objective 1.3: Enroll 400 eligible individuals in Medicaid or marketplace coverage
>
> **Goal 2: Reduce preventable chronic disease complications**
>
> - Objective 2.1: Provide ongoing care navigation to 200 individuals with newly diagnosed hypertension or diabetes
> - Objective 2.2: Achieve 75% medication adherence rate among care navigation participants
> - Objective 2.3: Reduce emergency department visits by 25% among enrolled participants

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Confusing activities with outcomes** — "hold 12 workshops" is an activity; "150 participants gain job skills" is an outcome
- **Overpromising** — unrealistic objectives undermine credibility
- **Vague methods** — "provide support to families" says nothing; specificity builds confidence
- **Ignoring implementation challenges** — acknowledge potential obstacles and how you'll address them

## Section 6: Evaluation Plan

**Purpose:** Show how you'll measure success and learn from the project. Funders want to know their investment will be tracked.

**Length:** 1-2 pages

### Template

```
EVALUATION PLAN

**Evaluation Approach**
[Brief description of your overall approach to evaluation—internal vs. external, formative vs. summative.]

**Outcomes and Indicators**

| Outcome | Indicator | Data Source | Collection Frequency |
|---------|-----------|-------------|---------------------|
| [What you expect to change] | [How you'll measure it] | [Where data comes from] | [How often collected] |

**Data Collection Methods**
[Description of tools and processes: surveys, interviews, administrative data, observations, etc.]

**Analysis and Reporting**
[How will data be analyzed? How often will you review results? How will findings be used?]

**External Evaluation** (if applicable)
[Description of external evaluator role and qualifications.]
```

### Example: Youth Mentoring Program

> **Evaluation Approach**
> We use a mixed-methods approach combining quantitative outcome tracking with qualitative feedback to assess both program effectiveness and participant experience.
>
> **Outcomes and Indicators**
>
> | Outcome | Indicator | Data Source | Collection Frequency |
> |---------|-----------|-------------|---------------------|
> | Improved academic performance | GPA change; course pass rate | School records (with consent) | Quarterly |
> | Increased school engagement | Attendance rate; disciplinary incidents | School records | Monthly |
> | Positive youth development | Developmental Assets Profile scores | Standardized survey | Pre/post |
> | Quality mentor relationships | Match satisfaction ratings | Program survey | Quarterly |
>
> **Data Collection Methods**
> - **Pre/post surveys:** All participants complete the Search Institute's Developmental Assets Profile at enrollment and program completion
> - **Academic records:** With parent consent, we obtain GPA and attendance data from partner schools
> - **Match tracking:** Mentors log meeting frequency, duration, and activities in our database
> - **Exit interviews:** 20% sample of completers participate in qualitative interviews
>
> **Analysis and Reporting**
> Program staff review outcome dashboards monthly. Quarterly reports summarize progress toward objectives and identify needed adjustments. Annual evaluation reports are shared with all funders and posted on our website.

### Tips for Small Nonprofits

You don't need expensive external evaluators or sophisticated databases. What matters is:

1. **Tracking the right things** — focus on outcomes, not just activities
2. **Collecting data consistently** — simple tracking is better than sporadic complex tracking
3. **Using what you learn** — show that evaluation informs program improvement

Simple tools like Google Forms for surveys, Excel for tracking, and regular staff review meetings are sufficient for most foundation grants.

### Budget Narrative Template

```
BUDGET NARRATIVE

**Personnel**
[Explain each position: what they'll do, why that level of effort is needed.]

The Program Coordinator (1.0 FTE, $52,000) will manage day-to-day program operations, including participant recruitment, activity coordination, and data collection. This is a new position required by the program expansion.

The Outreach Specialist (0.5 FTE, $21,000) will dedicate half-time to this project for community engagement, partnership development, and participant follow-up. This represents a portion of an existing position.

The Executive Director (0.1 FTE, $8,500) will provide oversight, funder relations, and strategic guidance. This allocation reflects supervisory time for the new program.

**Fringe Benefits**
Fringe benefits are calculated at 25% of salaries, covering health insurance, retirement contributions, and payroll taxes consistent with our organizational policies.

**Operations**
[Explain each line item.]

Program supplies ($5,000) include workbooks, art materials, and educational resources for 200 participants at $25 per participant.

Travel ($3,216) covers local transportation for home visits and community outreach, estimated at 400 miles per month at the federal rate of $0.67/mile.

**Indirect Costs**
Indirect costs of 15% cover facilities, utilities, administrative support, and organizational infrastructure. This rate is below our federally negotiated rate of 18.5%.
```

### Budget Red Flags to Avoid

- **Math errors** — double-check every calculation
- **Unrealistic salaries** — too low suggests you can't hire quality staff; too high raises questions
- **No indirect costs** — suggests inexperience; funders know programs have overhead
- **100% of a position on the grant** — suggests dependency; show diversified funding
- **Mismatched numbers** — budget totals must match executive summary and narrative

## Section 9: Sustainability Plan

**Purpose:** Show how the program will continue after the grant ends. Funders don't want to create dependency.

**Length:** 1 page

### Template

```
SUSTAINABILITY PLAN

**Continuing the Work**
[How will this program be funded after the grant period ends?]

**Revenue Strategies**
- [Strategy 1: e.g., government contracts, fee-for-service, individual donors]
- [Strategy 2]
- [Strategy 3]

**Organizational Capacity**
[How will this grant build capacity that outlasts the funding period?]

**Partnership Commitments**
[What partners have committed to ongoing support?]

**Timeline to Sustainability**
[When do you expect to reach self-sufficiency for this program?]
```

### Honest Approaches to Sustainability

Funders know that "we'll seek other grants" isn't a sustainability plan—it's grant dependency. Stronger approaches include:

- **Government contracts:** "Our success metrics will position us to compete for state prevention contracts in Year 3."
- **Fee-for-service:** "Participating schools will pay $50/student starting Year 2, covering 40% of program costs."
- **Integration:** "Successful pilot activities will be integrated into our core programming supported by annual fund."
- **Replication:** "We will develop a training curriculum allowing other organizations to implement this model."

Be honest. If the program requires ongoing grant support, say so—but show diversified sources.

## Section 10: Appendices and Attachments

**Purpose:** Provide supporting documentation without cluttering the main proposal.

### Standard Attachments

Most funders request some or all of:

**Required by nearly all funders:**
- IRS 501(c)(3) determination letter
- List of board members with affiliations
- Current year operating budget
- Most recent audited financial statements or Form 990

**Often requested:**
- Organizational chart
- Key staff resumes
- Letters of support from partners
- Logic model or theory of change diagram
- Annual report

**Sometimes requested:**
- Strategic plan
- Recent program evaluation
- Media coverage
- Client testimonials (with permission)

### Tips for Strong Letters of Support

Partner letters should be specific, not generic. A strong letter:

- States the specific role the partner will play
- Describes the history of the partnership
- Commits to concrete contributions (space, referrals, in-kind support)
- Is signed by someone with authority to make commitments

**Weak:** "We support this organization's important work."

**Strong:** "Jefferson County Schools commits to providing classroom space and recruiting 150 student participants for this after-school program. We have partnered with [Organization] since 2019 and have seen measurable improvements in participating students' academic engagement."

## Adapting Templates for Different Funder Types

### Private Foundations
- More flexibility in format
- Often appreciate storytelling and mission alignment
- May have informal processes—relationships matter
- Typically shorter proposals (5-10 pages)

### Government Grants
- Strict format requirements—follow exactly
- Emphasis on compliance and reporting capacity
- More detailed budgets with specific cost categories
- Often require specific assurances and certifications
- Longer proposals (20-50+ pages)

### Corporate Funders
- Focus on community impact and alignment with business priorities
- Often shorter proposals with clear metrics
- May emphasize partnership opportunities and visibility
- Quicker decisions, shorter funding cycles

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long should a grant proposal be?

Follow funder guidelines exactly. If no length is specified, aim for 8-12 pages for foundation grants (excluding attachments) and follow all page limits for government grants. Concise proposals that make strong cases beat lengthy proposals that pad content.

### Should I include a logic model?

Yes, if the funder requests it or if your program is complex enough to benefit from visual explanation. A one-page logic model showing inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes can clarify your theory of change more effectively than paragraphs of text.

### How do I handle a first-time application with no track record?

Emphasize: (1) relevant experience of staff and leadership, (2) track record of your partner organizations, (3) evidence base for your approach from research or similar programs elsewhere, and (4) pilot results if you've tested any elements.

### What if my budget doesn't match the funder's typical grant size?

If your project costs $150,000 and the funder typically gives $25,000 grants, request $25,000 and show your other funding sources. Funders prefer to be part of a funding coalition rather than sole supporters.

### Should I contact the funder before applying?

Usually yes. A brief email or call to the program officer can clarify priorities, confirm your project fits their guidelines, and establish a relationship. Don't pitch your project—ask questions about their interests and process.

### How do I handle rejection?

Request feedback. Many funders will share why your proposal wasn't funded. Common reasons include: program didn't match priorities, geographic area not served, budget issues, or simply more strong applications than funds available. Use feedback to strengthen future applications.

### Can I use AI tools to write grant proposals?

AI can help with drafting, editing, and brainstorming, but funders value authentic voice and specific local knowledge. Use AI as a starting point, then customize heavily with your organization's unique data, stories, and perspective. Generic AI-generated content is easy to spot and undermines credibility.

## Conclusion: Templates Are Starting Points

The templates in this guide give you structure, but the content must be authentically yours. The best grant proposals combine clear organization with compelling storytelling, specific local data, and genuine passion for the mission.

Remember that grant writing is fundamentally relationship-building. Funders aren't vending machines—they're people who want to invest in organizations they trust to make good use of resources. Your proposal is one part of that relationship.

Start with the template. Fill in your organization's unique story. Review with colleagues for clarity. And then submit—because the only unsuccessful proposal is the one you never send.
