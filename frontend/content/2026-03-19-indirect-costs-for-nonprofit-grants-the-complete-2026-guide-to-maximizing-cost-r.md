# Indirect Costs for Nonprofit Grants: The Complete 2026 Guide to Maximizing Cost Recovery

*Every nonprofit incurs overhead costs—rent, utilities, accounting, insurance. But too many organizations leave money on the table by not understanding how to recover these indirect costs through their federal grants. This guide shows you exactly how indirect cost recovery works, your options, and how to choose the right approach for your organization.*

## What Are Indirect Costs in Nonprofit Grants?

Indirect costs are the essential operating expenses that keep your nonprofit running but can't be tied directly to a single program or grant. Unlike direct costs—such as paying a program coordinator's salary or buying supplies for a specific project—indirect costs support everything your organization does.

### Common Examples of Indirect Costs

**Administrative and Management Costs:**
- Executive director salary (the portion spent on general management)
- Finance and accounting staff salaries
- Human resources personnel
- Office management

**Facilities Costs:**
- Rent or depreciation for office space
- Utilities (electricity, heating, internet)
- Building maintenance and cleaning
- Property insurance

**General Operating Expenses:**
- Annual audit fees
- General liability and directors & officers insurance
- Legal services for organizational matters
- IT infrastructure and support
- Board meeting expenses
- Office supplies for general use

### Direct Costs vs. Indirect Costs: Understanding the Difference

The distinction between direct and indirect costs isn't always obvious, and getting it wrong can create compliance problems.

**Direct costs** are expenses you can specifically identify with and charge to a particular grant or program. If you hire a case manager who works exclusively on one federally-funded program, their entire salary is a direct cost to that grant.

**Shared direct costs** are expenses that benefit multiple programs and need to be allocated across them. For example, if your program director splits their time equally between two grants, you would directly allocate 50% of their salary to each grant.

**Indirect costs** are expenses that benefit the entire organization and cannot be readily assigned to any specific program. Your CEO's time spent on overall organizational strategy, your annual audit, your general liability insurance—these support all programs equally but can't be practically divided among them.

Here's a practical test: If you could eliminate a specific program, would this cost disappear? If yes, it's likely direct. If the cost would remain regardless of which programs you operate, it's likely indirect.

## Why Indirect Cost Recovery Matters for Your Nonprofit

Many nonprofit leaders view indirect costs as a necessary evil or, worse, try to hide them from funders. This approach is both financially unsustainable and strategically misguided.

### The Hidden Subsidy Problem

When you don't recover indirect costs from your federal grants, you're essentially subsidizing those programs with your unrestricted funds. Every grant that doesn't cover its fair share of overhead drains resources that could go toward building organizational capacity, serving more clients, or maintaining adequate cash reserves.

Consider this scenario: Your nonprofit has a $500,000 federal grant for a youth development program. Your actual indirect cost rate (if you calculated it) would be 25% of modified total direct costs. But because you've never negotiated a rate, you're using the 15% de minimis rate—or worse, not claiming indirect costs at all.

That's $50,000 per year in overhead you're paying out of pocket—money that should be coming from the federal government.

### The Sustainability Imperative

Indirect costs aren't waste or inefficiency. They're the infrastructure that allows your programs to function. Organizations that chronically underfund overhead eventually face:

- Staff burnout from inadequate administrative support
- Technology failures from deferred IT investments
- Financial instability from lack of reserves
- Compliance problems from understaffed finance departments
- Leadership vacuums from below-market executive compensation

Recovering your full indirect costs isn't about padding your budget—it's about building an organization strong enough to fulfill its mission over the long term.

## Your Options for Indirect Cost Recovery on Federal Grants

When applying for or managing federal grants, you have three main options for recovering indirect costs. Each has its place depending on your organization's size, federal funding volume, and administrative capacity.

### Option 1: The 15% De Minimis Rate

The simplest option is the de minimis rate, which the Office of Management and Budget (OMB) increased from 10% to 15% of Modified Total Direct Costs (MTDC) effective October 1, 2024.

**Who can use it:**
- Any nonprofit that has never had a negotiated indirect cost rate agreement (NICRA)
- Organizations with an expired or terminated NICRA
- Organizations receiving less than $35 million in direct federal funding per year

**Advantages:**
- No documentation required to justify the rate
- No negotiation process with federal agencies
- Can be used indefinitely
- Provides predictable budgeting across all federal awards

**Disadvantages:**
- 15% is often lower than actual indirect cost rates
- Once you choose de minimis, you must use it consistently across all federal awards until you decide to negotiate a rate
- May significantly underfund your true overhead costs

**When to choose de minimis:**
- Your calculated indirect cost rate is at or below 15%
- You have limited federal funding and administrative capacity
- The NICRA process would cost more than you'd recover
- You're just starting to receive federal grants and want to keep things simple

### Option 2: Negotiated Indirect Cost Rate Agreement (NICRA)

A NICRA is a formal agreement between your organization and the federal government that establishes the indirect cost rate you can charge on all federal grants. Once negotiated with your cognizant agency, all other federal agencies must accept your NICRA rate.

**Who should consider it:**
- Organizations with significant federal funding
- Nonprofits whose true indirect cost rates exceed 15%
- Organizations managing multiple federal awards
- Those expanding programs and needing full cost recovery

**Advantages:**
- Recovers your actual indirect costs (rates of 20-35% or higher are common)
- Provides credibility and transparency with all funders
- Rate applies consistently across all federal awards
- Demonstrates financial management capability

**Disadvantages:**
- Requires detailed proposal and documentation
- Involves negotiation with your cognizant federal agency
- Requires ongoing maintenance and periodic renewal
- May invite more scrutiny from auditors

### Option 3: Waive Indirect Cost Recovery

You can choose not to claim indirect costs on federal awards. While generally not advisable, this option exists for organizations that:
- Have restricted funding that prohibits indirect cost charges
- Are using all grant funds for direct services by choice
- Are in special circumstances where indirect cost recovery isn't practical

## Understanding the NICRA: What Every Nonprofit Should Know

If your true indirect cost rate exceeds 15%, negotiating a NICRA is almost always worth the effort. Here's what you need to know about the process.

### What Is a NICRA?

A Negotiated Indirect Cost Rate Agreement is a legal document between your nonprofit and a federal agency that establishes:

- Your approved indirect cost rate
- The type of rate (provisional, predetermined, final, or fixed)
- The allocation base (usually MTDC or direct salaries and wages)
- The fiscal period(s) covered
- Any special conditions or limitations

Once you have a NICRA, all federal agencies must honor it. You won't need to negotiate separate rates with each funder.

### Finding Your Cognizant Agency

Your "cognizant agency for indirect costs" is the federal agency responsible for reviewing and approving your indirect cost rate proposal. This is typically the agency that provides the most direct federal funding to your organization.

**Important distinctions:**
- If you receive all federal funding as a subrecipient (through pass-through entities rather than directly from federal agencies), you're generally not eligible to negotiate a NICRA with a federal agency
- In this case, you can negotiate with your pass-through entity or use the de minimis rate
- The cognizant agency for indirect costs may be different from your cognizant agency for audit purposes

Common cognizant agencies include:
- Department of Health and Human Services (HHS)
- Department of Labor (DOL)
- National Science Foundation (NSF)
- USDA National Institute of Food and Agriculture (NIFA)
- Department of Education

Many agencies, including HHS, contract with the Department of Interior's Interior Business Center (IBC) to conduct indirect cost negotiations on their behalf.

### Types of Indirect Cost Rates

**Provisional Rate:** A temporary rate used for funding and interim reimbursement until a final rate is established. If your actual costs differ from the provisional rate, you'll need to make adjustments.

**Final Rate:** An audited rate that represents your actual indirect costs for a completed fiscal year. Established after the fiscal year closes and adjusts any differences from provisional rates.

**Predetermined Rate:** A fixed rate established for future fiscal periods based on historical costs. Not subject to adjustment during the period it covers.

**Fixed Rate:** Similar to predetermined, but with a "carry-forward" provision that adjusts future rates for any over- or under-recovery.

### What Goes Into the Indirect Cost Pool?

Your indirect cost rate is calculated as:

**Indirect Cost Pool ÷ Direct Cost Base = Indirect Cost Rate**

**The Indirect Cost Pool includes:**
- Administrative salaries not directly chargeable to programs
- General accounting, HR, and executive management
- General liability, D&O, and property insurance
- Audit fees
- Legal fees for general organizational matters
- Board expenses
- IT infrastructure supporting the whole organization
- Facilities costs (rent, utilities, maintenance)
- Office supplies for general use

**The Direct Cost Base (usually MTDC) includes:**
All direct costs except:
- Equipment and capital expenditures
- Rental costs (in some cases)
- Scholarships and fellowships
- Participant support costs
- The portion of subawards exceeding $50,000

### The NICRA Application Process

**Step 1: Confirm your cognizant agency**
Contact the agency you believe should be cognizant, or check each major federal funder's website. If you're unsure, many agencies provide guidance or contact information for assistance.

**Step 2: Gather required documentation**
Most agencies require:
- Organizational profile and chart
- Cost policy statement
- Audited financial statements
- Schedule of federal awards received
- Detailed indirect cost rate calculations
- Allocation of salaries by function
- Employee benefits breakdown
- Lobbying certificate
- Certificate of indirect costs (signed by authorized official)

**Step 3: Prepare your indirect cost rate proposal**
Follow your cognizant agency's format requirements. The Department of Interior's IBC provides templates that simplify preparation: https://ibc.doi.gov/ICS/indirect-cost

**Step 4: Submit your proposal**
Submit within six months of your fiscal year close, or within 90 days of receiving your first direct federal award.

**Step 5: Negotiate with the agency**
A federal negotiator will review your proposal and may request additional documentation. Negotiations typically take 4-6 months.

**Step 6: Sign the agreement**
Once you reach agreement, sign and return the NICRA. The rate takes effect as specified in the agreement.

### NICRA Timeline Requirements

| Fiscal Year End | Proposal Due By |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| September 30 | March 31 |
| December 31 | June 30 |
| June 30 | December 31 |

Organizations receiving $35 million or more in annual federal funding must negotiate rates annually and cannot use the de minimis option.

## Modified Total Direct Costs (MTDC): The Foundation of Indirect Cost Recovery

Whether you're using the de minimis rate or a negotiated rate, understanding Modified Total Direct Costs is essential.

### What MTDC Includes

MTDC starts with all direct costs and then excludes certain categories:

**Included in MTDC:**
- Direct salaries and wages
- Applicable fringe benefits
- Materials and supplies
- Services (consultants, contractors)
- Travel
- The first $50,000 of each subaward (regardless of period)

**Excluded from MTDC:**
- Equipment (generally items over $5,000)
- Capital expenditures
- Rental costs
- Charges for patient care
- Tuition remission
- Scholarships and fellowships
- Participant support costs
- The portion of subawards exceeding $50,000

### Calculating Indirect Cost Recovery: An Example

Let's walk through a practical calculation:

**Your federal grant budget:**
- Staff salaries: $200,000
- Fringe benefits: $50,000
- Supplies: $15,000
- Travel: $10,000
- Consultants: $25,000
- Subaward to partner organization: $80,000
- Equipment purchase: $20,000

**Step 1: Calculate MTDC**
Start with direct costs: $400,000

Exclude:
- Equipment: -$20,000
- Subaward above $50,000: -$30,000

MTDC = $350,000

**Step 2: Apply your indirect cost rate**

If using 15% de minimis:
$350,000 × 15% = $52,500 indirect cost recovery

If you have a 25% NICRA:
$350,000 × 25% = $87,500 indirect cost recovery

That's a $35,000 difference on a single grant. Over multiple grants and multiple years, negotiating a NICRA can mean hundreds of thousands of dollars in additional cost recovery.

## Cost Allocation Methods for Federal Awards

Before you can calculate an indirect cost rate, you need a system for allocating shared costs across your programs and funding sources. The Uniform Guidance (2 CFR Part 200) allows several methods.

### Simplified Allocation Method

Best for smaller organizations with a single major function or few federal awards.

**How it works:**
1. Separate total costs into direct and indirect categories
2. Divide total indirect costs by an equitable allocation base
3. Apply resulting rate to all federal awards

**Assumption:** All programs benefit equally from administrative support.

### Multiple Allocation Base Method

Best for organizations with diverse programs that receive varying levels of administrative support.

**How it works:**
1. Create separate indirect cost pools (e.g., facilities, administration)
2. Select an appropriate allocation base for each pool
3. Calculate separate rates for each pool
4. Apply each rate to relevant cost categories

**Common allocation bases:**
- Total direct costs
- Direct salaries and wages
- Direct salaries, wages, and fringe benefits
- Square footage (for facilities costs)
- FTE positions
- Number of transactions

### Direct Allocation Method

Used by organizations that treat nearly all costs as direct except general administration.

**How it works:**
1. Allocate most shared costs directly to programs using appropriate bases
2. Only general administration and general expenses remain as indirect
3. Calculate indirect rate using simplified method

**Required for:** Voluntary health and welfare organizations and comparable nonprofits that must follow specific accounting requirements.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Charging Costs Inconsistently

The federal government requires consistency in how you treat costs. You cannot charge accounting services as indirect on one grant and direct on another. You cannot charge equipment as a direct cost on federal grants while including depreciation in your indirect pool.

**Solution:** Develop a written cost policy statement that documents how each type of cost will be charged. Apply it consistently across all funding sources.

### Mistake 2: Including Unallowable Costs

Certain costs are explicitly unallowable under federal grants, whether charged directly or indirectly:

- Alcoholic beverages
- Entertainment costs
- Lobbying activities
- Fines and penalties
- Bad debts
- Contributions to contingency reserves
- Fundraising costs (with limited exceptions)
- Executive compensation above federal thresholds

Including unallowable costs in your indirect pool will trigger audit findings and potential repayments.

### Mistake 3: Not Documenting Your Allocation Methods

Auditors will ask how you calculated your indirect cost rate and allocated shared costs. Without written documentation, you're vulnerable to audit findings and could lose the ability to recover indirect costs.

**Solution:** Maintain a written cost allocation plan that describes:
- How costs are classified (direct vs. indirect)
- What allocation bases you use
- How you calculate and apply rates
- Who reviews and approves allocations

### Mistake 4: Misunderstanding Indirect vs. Administrative Caps

Some federal programs cap "administrative costs" at a certain percentage. This is not the same as capping indirect costs.

Administrative costs can include both direct and indirect components:
- A program manager's salary (direct, but administrative)
- General accounting services (indirect)

If a program caps administrative costs at 10%, you need to understand exactly what counts toward that cap—it may or may not include all of your indirect rate.

### Mistake 5: Assuming the De Minimis Rate Is Your Only Option

Many smaller nonprofits default to the 15% de minimis rate without ever calculating their actual indirect cost rate. Some discover too late that their true rate is 25%, 30%, or higher.

**Solution:** Calculate your actual rate before committing to de minimis. If it's significantly higher than 15% and you have substantial federal funding, the NICRA process is likely worth the investment.

## Special Considerations for 2026

### October 2024 Uniform Guidance Updates

The 2024 revisions to the Uniform Guidance brought several important changes:

**De minimis rate increase:** The rate rose from 10% to 15% of MTDC, effective October 1, 2024. The new rate applies to awards executed after that date. Existing awards may apply the 15% rate to costs incurred after October 1, 2024, if sufficient funds remain.

**Switching from NICRA to de minimis:** Organizations with expired or terminated NICRAs can now use the de minimis rate. Previously, having any history of a negotiated rate barred organizations from de minimis.

**MTDC threshold changes:** The subaward exclusion threshold remains at the first $50,000.

### Federal Funding Landscape

Recent federal workforce reductions have affected NICRA processing times at some agencies. Plan ahead and:
- Submit proposals well before deadlines
- Be patient during negotiations
- Document all communications
- Consider whether temporary provisional rates might help

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I switch from a NICRA to the de minimis rate?

Yes, but only after your NICRA expires or is terminated. You cannot use the de minimis rate while you have an active NICRA. If you want to switch, contact your cognizant agency about terminating your agreement.

### Does my NICRA apply to foundation grants too?

Non-federal funders are not required to accept your NICRA. However, having a federally approved rate can boost credibility with foundations and demonstrate that your indirect cost calculations have been vetted. Many foundations respect NICRAs even if they don't fund at the full rate.

### What if my indirect cost rate is higher than what funders will pay?

Keep two sets of records:
1. Your actual indirect cost rate (calculated using your true costs)
2. The rate each funder allows

Book your true rate against all programs in your accounting system. This reveals which programs are covering their full costs and which require subsidy from unrestricted funds.

### Can faith-based organizations apply for a NICRA?

Yes. Faith-based organizations that don't file IRS Form 990 can still negotiate a NICRA if they meet federal requirements and have appropriate financial documentation. An audited financial statement may be required.

### What happens if my provisional rate differs from my final rate?

You'll need to make adjustments. If your final rate is lower than provisional, you may need to return funds. If higher, you generally cannot recover additional costs unless funds remain in the award. This is why predetermined rates are preferred when possible.

### How often do I need to renew my NICRA?

This depends on your rate type and cognizant agency. Generally:
- Organizations receiving $35M+ in federal funds annually must renew every year
- Others may negotiate multi-year rates
- Rate extensions of up to 4 years are available for eligible organizations

## Your Indirect Cost Action Plan

### Step 1: Calculate Your True Indirect Cost Rate

Before making any decisions, know where you stand:
1. Gather your most recent audited financial statements
2. Identify all indirect costs
3. Determine your MTDC or other allocation base
4. Calculate: Indirect Costs ÷ Base = Rate

### Step 2: Compare Your Options

| Factor | De Minimis (15%) | NICRA |
|--------|------------------|-------|
| Your calculated rate | At or below 15% | Above 15% |
| Federal funding volume | Less than $500K/year | More than $500K/year |
| Administrative capacity | Limited | Adequate |
| Number of federal awards | 1-2 | Multiple |
| Staff expertise | Basic | Finance/compliance staff |

### Step 3: Develop Your Cost Allocation Plan

Whether you pursue a NICRA or use de minimis, document your approach:
- Define direct vs. indirect costs for your organization
- Select allocation bases for shared costs
- Create procedures for monthly allocation
- Establish review and approval processes

### Step 4: Implement and Monitor

- Apply your rate consistently across all federal awards
- Track indirect cost recovery against budget
- Monitor for any over- or under-recovery
- Adjust future proposals based on actual experience

### Step 5: Review Annually

Each year:
- Recalculate your actual indirect cost rate
- Assess whether your current approach is optimal
- Consider whether to pursue or renew a NICRA
- Update cost allocation procedures as needed

## Conclusion

Indirect cost recovery isn't about maximizing what you extract from federal grants—it's about sustainable nonprofit management. Federal funders understand that programs don't operate in a vacuum. They expect grantees to have indirect costs and provide mechanisms for recovering them.

Your job is to understand these mechanisms, calculate your true costs, and choose the approach that best supports your organization's mission. For many nonprofits, that means moving beyond the 15% de minimis rate and negotiating a NICRA that reflects actual overhead.

The investment of time and resources in proper indirect cost management pays dividends: stronger financial health, better compliance, enhanced credibility, and ultimately, greater capacity to serve your community.
