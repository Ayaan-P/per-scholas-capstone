You've submitted your grant proposal. Now it enters a process you've probably never seen from the inside: the review. Understanding how funders actually evaluate proposals isn't just curiosity—it's strategy. When you know exactly what reviewers are looking for (and what makes them reach for the reject pile), you can write proposals that win.

Here's what really happens after you click "submit."

## The Anatomy of Grant Review

### Who's Actually Reading Your Proposal?

Depending on the funder, your proposal might be reviewed by:

**Program Officers** (Foundation Grants)
- Deep expertise in the funding area
- Focus on strategic fit with funder priorities
- Consider long-term relationship potential
- Usually 1-3 reviewers per proposal

**Peer Reviewers** (Government Grants)
- Subject matter experts from the field
- Score using standardized rubrics
- Often 3-5 reviewers per proposal
- Scores get averaged or discussed in panels

**Board Members** (Community Foundations)
- Business and community leaders
- May have limited technical expertise
- Focus on community impact and credibility
- Often make final decisions on recommendations

**The key insight:** Write for your actual audience. A NIH proposal needs scientific rigor. A community foundation proposal needs accessibility. Same organization, same program—different proposals.

## The Scoring Rubric: What Really Matters

Most funders use some version of weighted criteria. Here's a typical breakdown:

### Federal Grant Scoring (Example)
| Criteria | Weight |
|----------|--------|
| Statement of Need | 15% |
| Goals and Objectives | 15% |
| Program Design/Methods | 25% |
| Organizational Capacity | 15% |
| Evaluation Plan | 15% |
| Budget/Cost Effectiveness | 15% |

### Foundation Scoring (Typical)
| Criteria | Weight |
|----------|--------|
| Alignment with Mission | 25% |
| Demonstrated Need | 20% |
| Feasibility | 20% |
| Expected Impact | 20% |
| Organizational Capacity | 15% |

**The insight:** Most proposals fail on feasibility and evaluation, not need or alignment. Funders already believe in your cause—they need to believe you can actually do what you're proposing.

## What Makes Reviewers Say "Yes"

After interviewing program officers and reviewing thousands of proposals, patterns emerge. Here's what gets high scores:

### 1. Clear, Specific Problem Statements

**Low Score:**
> "Homelessness is a growing problem in our community that affects many people."

**High Score:**
> "In 2025, 847 individuals experienced unsheltered homelessness in Springfield—a 34% increase since 2022. Of these, 62% report untreated mental health conditions as a contributing factor, yet our county has only 12 emergency psychiatric beds."

**Why:** Specificity signals expertise. Vague problems suggest you don't understand the issue deeply enough to solve it.

### 2. SMART Objectives with Measurable Outcomes

**Low Score:**
> "We will reduce homelessness in our community."

**High Score:**
> "By December 2027, we will permanently house 120 chronically homeless individuals (40% of current population) with 85% maintaining housing at 12-month follow-up."

**Why:** Reviewers need to evaluate whether you'll actually accomplish something. Vague goals are unfundable because they're unmeasurable.

### 3. Evidence-Based Methods

**Low Score:**
> "Our innovative approach combines case management with housing support."

**High Score:**
> "We implement Housing First using the evidence-based Pathways model, which randomized controlled trials show achieves 80% housing retention vs. 30% for traditional approaches (Tsemberis et al., 2004). Our pilot data shows 78% retention at 6 months."

**Why:** "Innovative" is a red flag if it means "untested." Funders want confidence their investment will work.

### 4. Realistic Budgets with Justification

**Low Score:**
> "Personnel: $150,000"

**High Score:**
> "Program Director (1.0 FTE): $65,000 + $19,500 benefits (30%)
> The Program Director oversees all case management, maintains funder relationships, and ensures data quality. Salary is benchmarked to 75th percentile for comparable roles in our metro area to ensure recruitment and retention."

**Why:** Detailed budgets show you've actually thought through implementation. Lumpy budgets suggest you're guessing.

### 5. Honest Risk Assessment

**Low Score:**
> "We are confident this program will succeed."

**High Score:**
> "Key risks include: (1) Affordable housing shortage may limit placement options—mitigated through landlord incentive program and partnerships with 3 property management companies; (2) Staff turnover in homeless services averages 30%—mitigated through competitive wages (15% above sector average) and monthly supervision."

**Why:** Every program has risks. Proposals that acknowledge none appear naive. Proposals that address risks appear sophisticated.

## What Makes Reviewers Say "No"

Here are the most common reasons proposals get rejected, in order of frequency:

### 1. Doesn't Match Funder Priorities (45% of rejections)

The proposal is fine, but it's sent to the wrong funder. This is the single biggest waste in grant writing.

**Prevention:** Read guidelines obsessively. Look at past grantees. If uncertain, call the program officer.

### 2. Lack of Organizational Capacity (20% of rejections)

The idea is good, but reviewers don't believe this organization can execute it.

**Signs reviewers look for:**
- Prior relevant experience
- Staff qualifications
- Financial stability (990s, audits)
- Track record with similar grants
- Partnership letters (real ones, not generic)

**Prevention:** If you lack capacity, partner with someone who has it. A joint proposal with a credible lead is stronger than a solo proposal with capacity gaps.

### 3. Weak Evaluation Plan (15% of rejections)

Funders increasingly demand evidence their money accomplished something. Proposals that can't explain how they'll measure success get passed over.

**What reviewers want:**
- Logic model or theory of change
- Specific data collection methods
- Realistic evaluation timeline
- How findings will be used

**Prevention:** Budget for evaluation (typically 5-10% of program costs). Consider external evaluators for larger grants.

### 4. Unrealistic Budget (10% of rejections)

Either too high (padding), too low (naive), or misaligned with activities described.

**Red flags:**
- Personnel costs that don't match described FTEs
- No indirect costs (suggests inexperience with government grants)
- Round numbers everywhere
- No matching funds when required

**Prevention:** Have someone with grant budget experience review before submission.

### 5. Poor Writing Quality (10% of rejections)

Reviewers read dozens of proposals. If yours is hard to follow, it gets lower scores even if content is good.

**Instant credibility kills:**
- Typos in the executive summary
- Cut-and-paste errors from other proposals
- Exceeding page limits
- Answering questions out of order
- Inconsistent numbers between narrative and budget

**Prevention:** Have two fresh readers review before submission. Print it out—you'll catch errors you miss on screen.

## The Psychology of Grant Reviewers

Understanding reviewer psychology improves your proposals:

### They're Overwhelmed

A federal reviewer might read 30 proposals in a week. Yours is competing for attention.

**Implication:** Front-load your best material. Use headers, bullets, and white space. Make key points scannable.

### They're Skeptical

Reviewers have seen beautiful proposals that went nowhere. They're looking for red flags.

**Implication:** Acknowledge limitations. Provide evidence. Be specific. Vague optimism triggers skepticism.

### They're Human

Reviewers want to fund good work. They're looking for reasons to say yes, not just reasons to say no.

**Implication:** Make it easy for them to champion your proposal. Give them quotable impact statements. Make them believe.

### They Remember Relationships

For foundation grants especially, proposals from known organizations with good track records get the benefit of the doubt.

**Implication:** Build relationships before you need money. Attend funder events. Send updates on past grants. Be a good partner.

## The Review Process Timeline

Understanding when decisions happen helps you follow up appropriately:

### Federal Grants (typical)
- **0-2 weeks:** Administrative review (eligibility check)
- **2-8 weeks:** Peer review (scoring)
- **8-12 weeks:** Panel discussion (ranking)
- **12-20 weeks:** Program office recommendation
- **20-26 weeks:** Award notification

### Foundation Grants (typical)
- **0-2 weeks:** Staff initial screen
- **2-4 weeks:** Program officer review
- **4-8 weeks:** Due diligence calls
- **8-12 weeks:** Board meeting (quarterly cycle)
- **12-16 weeks:** Award notification

**When to follow up:** Two weeks after stated decision date is appropriate. One email, polite, asking about timeline.

## Using This Knowledge to Win

Here's how to apply these insights to your next proposal:

### Before Writing
1. Confirm true alignment with funder priorities (call if uncertain)
2. Research who reviews and what they value
3. Get the scoring rubric if available
4. Review winning proposals if the funder shares them

### While Writing
1. Mirror the rubric structure in your narrative
2. Make every claim evidence-based
3. Anticipate and address objections
4. Budget realistically with full justification

### Before Submitting
1. Score your own proposal against the rubric
2. Have an external reader flag unclear sections
3. Check consistency between narrative and budget
4. Verify all requirements are met

### After Submitting
1. Request reviewer feedback (win or lose)
2. Track patterns across submissions
3. Revise based on feedback for resubmission
