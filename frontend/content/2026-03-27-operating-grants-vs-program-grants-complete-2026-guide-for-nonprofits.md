# Operating Grants vs Program Grants: The Complete 2026 Guide for Nonprofits

Understanding the difference between operating grants and program grants is one of the most important funding decisions your nonprofit will make. These two grant types serve fundamentally different purposes—and choosing the right mix can determine whether your organization thrives or merely survives.

This comprehensive guide breaks down everything you need to know about operating grants versus program grants, including when to pursue each type, how they affect your budget, and strategies for building a balanced funding portfolio.

## What Are Operating Grants?

Operating grants—also called general operating support (GOS), unrestricted grants, or core support grants—provide flexible funding that nonprofits can allocate wherever needed most. Unlike funding tied to specific initiatives, operating grants cover the day-to-day expenses that keep your organization running.

### What Operating Grants Cover

Operating grants typically fund:

- **Staff salaries and benefits** - From executive leadership to program coordinators
- **Rent and utilities** - Office space, electricity, heating, internet
- **Administrative costs** - Accounting, HR, legal, insurance
- **Technology and infrastructure** - Computers, software, phone systems
- **Professional development** - Staff training, conferences, certifications
- **Fundraising operations** - Development staff, donor management systems
- **General supplies** - Office equipment, materials, printing

The key characteristic of operating grants is flexibility. When your nonprofit receives a $50,000 operating grant, your leadership team decides how to deploy those funds based on organizational priorities—not funder requirements.

### Why Operating Grants Matter

According to Grantmakers for Effective Organizations (GEO), general operating support is "the working capital nonprofits need to sustain their day-to-day operations." Without adequate operating support, organizations may struggle to meet basic needs like payroll and rent—regardless of how many program grants they've secured.

The reality is stark: most nonprofits allocate 65-85% of their budget to program-specific expenses, leaving 15-35% for everything else. Operating grants fill that critical gap.

## What Are Program Grants?

Program grants—also called project grants, restricted grants, or designated grants—provide funding tied to specific initiatives, activities, or outcomes. These grants support particular programs rather than general organizational operations.

### What Program Grants Cover

Program grants typically fund:

- **Direct service delivery** - The actual work of your programs
- **Program staff salaries** - Employees working directly on the funded initiative
- **Program supplies and materials** - Resources needed to deliver services
- **Participant costs** - Stipends, transportation, meals for beneficiaries
- **Evaluation and measurement** - Tracking outcomes for the specific program
- **Program-specific equipment** - Items used exclusively for the funded project

Program grants come with strings attached. If a foundation awards $100,000 to expand your after-school tutoring program, you must spend those funds on tutoring activities—not office rent or executive salaries.

### Why Program Grants Dominate the Funding Landscape

Program grants remain the most common form of foundation funding for several reasons:

1. **Measurable outcomes** - Funders can track specific results
2. **Clear alignment** - Easy to connect grants to foundation priorities
3. **Donor satisfaction** - Major donors often want to fund visible programs
4. **Reduced risk** - Funders have more control over how money is spent
5. **Compelling stories** - Programs generate beneficiary narratives

For these reasons, many nonprofits find themselves chasing program grants even when operating support would better serve their needs.

## Operating Grants vs Program Grants: Key Differences

Understanding the core differences between these grant types helps you make strategic funding decisions.

### Flexibility and Restrictions

**Operating grants** offer maximum flexibility. Your organization determines spending priorities based on current needs. If an unexpected expense arises—say, your accounting software needs emergency replacement—you can redirect funds accordingly.

**Program grants** restrict spending to designated activities. Deviating from the approved budget typically requires funder approval. If you discover mid-project that you need different supplies than originally planned, you may need to request a budget modification.

### Reporting Requirements

**Operating grants** generally require less detailed reporting. Funders want to know your organization remained financially healthy and continued pursuing its mission. Reports might include annual financials, a narrative update on organizational progress, and basic metrics.

**Program grants** demand specific outcome reporting. You'll track participants served, activities completed, and measurable results. Quarterly reports with detailed expense documentation are common. Some funders require logic models, evaluation data, and participant testimonials.

### Relationship Dynamics

**Operating grants** signal trust. When a foundation provides unrestricted support, they're saying: "We believe in your leadership and mission. Use these funds wisely." This often indicates a mature funder-grantee relationship.

**Program grants** reflect traditional philanthropy dynamics. The funder identifies a problem they want solved, and the nonprofit proposes a solution. The relationship is more transactional—funding in exchange for specific deliverables.

### Impact on Organizational Health

**Operating grants** directly strengthen organizations. They allow investment in infrastructure, staff development, and systems that improve long-term effectiveness. Organizations with adequate operating support can respond to crises, seize opportunities, and plan strategically.

**Program grants** can inadvertently harm organizational health when they dominate the funding mix. Nonprofits may design programs to match funder priorities rather than community needs. They might underfund overhead, leading to staff burnout and infrastructure decay.

## The Real Cost Problem: Why Program Grants Alone Don't Work

Here's the uncomfortable truth many grant-funded nonprofits face: program grants rarely cover the full cost of programs.

### The Overhead Fiction

When a nonprofit applies for a program grant, they submit a budget showing direct costs (staff time, supplies, participant expenses) and indirect costs (rent, utilities, administration). Many funders cap indirect cost rates at 10-15%—sometimes less.

But the actual overhead rate for most nonprofits ranges from 25-40% of total expenses. This means program grants systematically underfund the infrastructure that makes programs possible.

Consider this scenario:

- A foundation awards $100,000 for your youth mentoring program
- They allow 15% indirect costs ($15,000)
- Your actual overhead rate is 30%
- The program actually costs $130,000 when fully loaded
- You're underfunded by $15,000 from day one

Multiply this across multiple program grants, and you've created a structural deficit. Each new program grant makes the problem worse.

### Mission Drift Risk

When organizations chase program grants to survive, they risk drifting from their core mission. A food bank might apply for a nutrition education grant because the funding is available—even though distribution remains their core strength. An arts organization might pursue STEM programming because funders prioritize STEM—diluting their artistic mission.

Operating grants reduce this pressure. With a solid operating foundation, nonprofits can pursue program funding that genuinely aligns with mission rather than funding that simply keeps the lights on.

## The Case for Operating Grants

Grantmakers for Effective Organizations makes a compelling case for why foundations should provide more general operating support:

### Enabling Strong Infrastructure

"Nonprofits can use this unrestricted funding as they see fit to address urgent and emerging issues, boost salaries and benefits, invest in technology and other infrastructure, strengthen communications and fundraising efforts and meet other operational needs."

Organizations with strong infrastructure deliver better programs. It's that simple.

### Reducing Burnout

When nonprofits constantly scramble for restricted funding, executive directors and development staff burn out. They spend excessive time on grant writing and reporting rather than mission delivery. Operating support eases this pressure.

### Fostering Innovation

Operating grants provide bandwidth for innovation and risk-taking. When basic operations are secure, organizations can pilot new approaches, respond to emerging opportunities, and iterate on programs. Restricted funding forces conservative, pre-approved activities.

### Reducing Power Imbalances

GEO notes that operating support "reduces the power imbalance between grantmaker and grantee by allowing nonprofits to devise their own solutions based on their vast experience in the community and field."

Nonprofits know their communities best. Operating support lets them apply that knowledge without funder micromanagement.

## The Case for Program Grants

Program grants aren't villains in the nonprofit funding story. They serve important purposes:

### Demonstrating Accountability

Program grants create clear accountability structures. Funders know exactly what they're buying, and nonprofits commit to specific deliverables. This transparency benefits both parties.

### Building Funder Relationships

Many foundation relationships begin with program grants. A new grantee demonstrates competence by successfully delivering a funded program. This track record opens doors to operating support later.

### Scaling Specific Initiatives

When a nonprofit wants to significantly expand a particular program, targeted program funding makes sense. Operating grants typically aren't large enough to launch major new initiatives.

### Matching Funder Priorities

Some foundations have specific focus areas that align perfectly with your programs. If the Johnson Foundation exclusively funds environmental education and you run environmental education programs, pursue that alignment.

### Creating Programmatic Clarity

Program grants force organizations to think clearly about costs, activities, and outcomes. This discipline benefits organizational planning even when it creates restrictions.

## Building a Balanced Funding Portfolio

The healthiest nonprofits don't choose between operating grants and program grants—they build portfolios incorporating both.

### The Ideal Mix

While there's no universal formula, many consultants recommend:

- **25-35% unrestricted/operating support** - Covers overhead and provides flexibility
- **50-65% program grants** - Funds core service delivery
- **10-15% earned revenue, events, individual donors** - Diversifies beyond grants

Organizations with less than 20% unrestricted funding often struggle with cash flow and infrastructure. Those approaching 40%+ unrestricted support typically demonstrate exceptional organizational health.

### Strategies for Increasing Operating Support

If your portfolio skews heavily toward program grants, consider these strategies:

**1. Cultivate Multi-Year Relationships**

Foundations typically provide operating support to organizations they know well. Invest in relationship-building with program officers. Deliver excellent results on initial grants. Communicate proactively about organizational needs.

**2. Frame the Ask Appropriately**

When seeking operating support, demonstrate:
- Organizational stability and governance
- Clear theory of change
- Evidence of impact across multiple programs
- Plans for sustainability
- Transparency about actual overhead costs

**3. Target Operating-Focused Funders**

Some foundations explicitly prioritize general operating support:
- Ford Foundation
- MacArthur Foundation
- W.K. Kellogg Foundation
- Many community foundations

Research funder priorities before applying. Don't request operating support from foundations that exclusively make program grants.

**4. Negotiate Indirect Cost Rates**

When applying for program grants, push back on artificially low indirect rates. Provide documentation of your actual overhead costs. Some funders will negotiate higher rates when presented with evidence.

**5. Build Reserves Through Operating Support**

When you receive operating grants, consider allocating a portion to reserves rather than immediately spending everything. This builds financial resilience over time.

## How Funders Think About This Choice

Understanding funder perspectives helps you craft stronger proposals for both grant types.

### Why Funders Provide Operating Support

Foundations that prioritize operating support typically believe:

- Strong organizations produce strong outcomes
- Nonprofit leaders understand community needs best
- Excessive restrictions waste time and resources
- Trust-based philanthropy creates better partnerships
- Infrastructure investment multiplies program effectiveness

When approaching these funders, emphasize organizational capacity, leadership quality, and holistic impact.

### Why Funders Prefer Program Support

Foundations that prioritize program support typically believe:

- Measurable outcomes demonstrate stewardship
- Strategic alignment ensures mission fit
- Accountability mechanisms protect charitable dollars
- Program focus prevents mission drift
- Specific interventions create tangible change

When approaching these funders, emphasize clear outcomes, evaluation plans, and alignment with their strategic priorities.

### The Overhead Myth

GEO explicitly addresses common misconceptions:

**Myth:** "We shouldn't be supporting our grantees' operating costs."
**Reality:** Like other organizations, nonprofits need working capital to succeed. If they underinvest in salaries and other infrastructure costs, they will eventually hollow out their finances, strain capacity and end up less effective.

**Myth:** "This will only encourage grantees to increase spending on salaries and other administrative costs."
**Reality:** Cases of exorbitant nonprofit staff salaries are few and far between. The reality is that most nonprofits pay under the market rate for their staff.

**Myth:** "We're adequately supporting nonprofit infrastructure through the overhead associated with our project grants."
**Reality:** Grantmakers rarely cover all associated direct and indirect costs of funded projects. The percentages grantmakers allow are often arbitrary and too low.

## Program Grant Best Practices

When pursuing program grants, set yourself up for success:

### Budget Accurately

Include all actual costs, including:
- Fully-loaded staff time (salary + benefits + payroll taxes)
- Proportional overhead allocations
- Evaluation costs
- Administrative time for reporting
- Indirect costs at your actual rate (request it even if capped)

### Define Clear Outcomes

Articulate what success looks like with specificity:
- How many people will you serve?
- What changes will participants experience?
- How will you measure these changes?
- What's your timeline for results?

### Plan for Sustainability

Most funders want to know what happens when grant funding ends. Address:
- How will the program continue?
- What other funding sources will you pursue?
- Can the program generate earned revenue?
- Will it eventually integrate into operating costs?

### Build in Learning

Include evaluation and learning components:
- How will you know if the program works?
- What adjustments might you make mid-course?
- How will you share learnings with the field?

## Operating Grant Best Practices

When pursuing operating grants, demonstrate organizational strength:

### Show Financial Health

Provide:
- Audited financial statements
- Board-approved budgets
- Cash flow projections
- Diversified revenue sources
- Reasonable reserves

### Articulate Strategic Direction

Explain:
- Where is the organization heading?
- What priorities drive resource allocation?
- How does operating support advance the mission?
- What would unrestricted funding enable that restricted funding cannot?

### Demonstrate Impact Across Programs

Operating support funds the whole organization, so show:
- Impact across your program portfolio
- How programs connect and reinforce each other
- Organizational-level outcomes beyond individual projects

### Be Transparent About Needs

Operating grant applications benefit from honesty:
- What infrastructure investments would strengthen your organization?
- Where are you under-resourced?
- What would adequate operating support enable?

## Finding Operating Grant Opportunities

Operating grants are sometimes called "unicorn grants" because they're harder to find than program support. Here's where to look:

### Community Foundations

Local and regional community foundations often provide operating support to established nonprofits. Check your area's community foundation website for grant opportunities.

### Corporate Foundations

Some corporate foundations—particularly those emphasizing community investment—provide unrestricted support. Walmart Foundation, Wells Fargo Foundation, and others offer operating grants in their communities.

### National Foundations

Large national foundations increasingly prioritize operating support:
- Ford Foundation
- MacArthur Foundation
- W.K. Kellogg Foundation
- Open Society Foundations

### State Arts and Cultural Agencies

State arts councils frequently offer general operating support to cultural organizations. California Arts Council, Illinois Arts Council, and Georgia Council for the Arts all provide GOS grants.

### Grant Databases

Use filtering tools in grant databases:
- On Instrumentl, select "General Operating Expense" under funding use
- On GrantWatch, browse the "Operating Support" category
- On Candid, filter by support type

## Hybrid Approaches

Some funding structures blend operating and program characteristics:

### Capacity Building Grants

Capacity building grants fund specific organizational improvements—strategic planning, board development, technology upgrades—rather than general operations. They're restricted to capacity activities but strengthen the organization overall.

### Core Support Grants

Some funders use "core support" to mean substantial operating support focused on organizational health. The term sometimes implies multi-year commitments.

### Flexible Program Grants

Progressive funders increasingly provide program grants with flexibility built in. They might allow budget modifications without approval or encourage grantees to redirect funds as needs evolve.

### Multi-Year Operating Support

Some foundations provide two-to-five-year operating support commitments. This extended timeline provides stability for organizational planning while maintaining funder engagement.

## FAQ: Operating Grants vs Program Grants

### Which type of grant is easier to get?

Program grants are generally easier to secure, especially for organizations without established funder relationships. Operating grants typically require demonstrated track records and trust-based relationships with foundations.

### Can small nonprofits get operating support?

Yes, though it's more challenging. Small organizations should pursue operating support from community foundations, local funders, and corporate giving programs. Building a multi-year relationship with one foundation often leads to operating support.

### What's the average operating grant size?

Operating grant sizes vary dramatically based on funder capacity and organizational budget. Small community foundation grants might be $5,000-$25,000. Major foundation operating grants can exceed $500,000 annually.

### How do I report on an operating grant?

Operating grant reporting typically includes: annual financial statements, a narrative describing organizational activities and achievements, basic outcome metrics across programs, and information about how operating support strengthened the organization.

### Can I apply for both operating and program support from the same funder?

Some foundations accept applications for both types simultaneously, while others limit grantees to one type per cycle. Check funder guidelines. If pursuing both, clearly differentiate what each grant would fund.

### Do program grants hurt my operating budget?

Potentially. If program grants don't cover full indirect costs, each new program grant can create structural deficits. Calculate your true indirect rate and compare it to what funders allow.

### What percentage of my budget should be operating support?

Financial advisors typically recommend 25-35% unrestricted support for organizational health. Organizations with less than 20% unrestricted funding often struggle; those above 35% demonstrate exceptional financial resilience.

## Conclusion: Building a Sustainable Funding Strategy

The operating grants versus program grants debate isn't about choosing a winner. Both grant types serve essential purposes in nonprofit funding.

Program grants enable specific initiatives, create accountability, and often provide the initial relationships that lead to deeper funder partnerships. Operating grants provide flexibility, strengthen infrastructure, and signal funder trust in organizational leadership.

The most successful nonprofits build balanced portfolios incorporating both types. They pursue program grants for expansion initiatives while cultivating operating support for organizational stability. They negotiate fair indirect cost rates and advocate for the true cost of programs. They invest in funder relationships that evolve from transactional program support toward partnership-based operating support.

Your organization's optimal funding mix depends on your size, maturity, program portfolio, and funder relationships. But regardless of where you start, working toward greater unrestricted support should be a strategic priority.

Strong organizations deliver strong programs. Operating support builds strong organizations. The connection is that simple—and that important.
