# How to Write a Grant Budget: The Complete Guide for Nonprofits

A perfectly written grant proposal can still get rejected if the budget doesn't add up. Funders scrutinize budgets closely—they're looking for realistic numbers, clear justifications, and evidence that you've thought through every dollar.

This guide covers everything you need to know about writing grant budgets that get approved.

## Why Grant Budgets Matter More Than You Think

Your budget tells funders two things:

1. **Can you actually execute this project?** Unrealistic budgets signal inexperience.
2. **Do you understand your own costs?** Vague or inflated numbers raise red flags.

Many program officers review the budget *before* reading the narrative. If the numbers don't make sense, they may not read further.

## The Two Types of Grant Budgets

### Line-Item Budget
A detailed breakdown of every expense category. Most funders require this format.

Example:
```
Personnel
  Project Director (0.5 FTE)          $35,000
  Program Coordinator (1.0 FTE)       $42,000
  Benefits (25%)                      $19,250
  
Subtotal Personnel                    $96,250

Program Expenses
  Training Materials                  $2,500
  Participant Supplies                $4,000
  Event Space Rental                  $3,600
  
Subtotal Program                      $10,100

Administrative/Indirect (15%)         $15,952

TOTAL PROJECT COST                   $122,302
```

### Functional Budget
Groups expenses by activity or objective. Less common but useful for complex, multi-phase projects.

## Essential Budget Categories

### 1. Personnel Costs
Usually the largest category. Include:

- **Salaries**: Calculate based on FTE (full-time equivalent) allocation to the project
- **Benefits**: Typically 20-35% of salary (health insurance, retirement, payroll taxes)
- **Consultants/Contractors**: If using external expertise

**Pro tip**: Funders want to see realistic salaries. Check comparable positions on GuideStar or Indeed.

### 2. Fringe Benefits
Break this out separately if your funder requires it:

- FICA/payroll taxes: ~7.65%
- Health insurance: varies widely
- Retirement contributions: typically 3-6%
- Workers' compensation: varies by state
- Unemployment insurance: ~1-3%

### 3. Equipment
Items over $5,000 (or your organization's capitalization threshold). Examples:
- Computers and software
- Vehicles
- Specialized machinery

Note: Many funders won't cover equipment purchases. Check the guidelines carefully.

### 4. Supplies
Consumable items under your equipment threshold:
- Office supplies
- Program materials
- Postage
- Printing

### 5. Travel
Be specific:
- **Local travel**: Mileage at IRS rate (currently $0.67/mile for 2026)
- **Out-of-town travel**: Airfare, hotel, per diem, ground transportation
- **Conference attendance**: Registration fees plus travel

### 6. Contractual Services
Third-party services:
- Evaluation consultants
- Trainers
- IT support
- Accounting services

### 7. Indirect Costs (F&A)

Indirect costs cover organizational overhead not directly attributable to the project:
- Rent and utilities
- Administrative staff
- General insurance
- IT infrastructure

**Important**: Federal grants allow negotiated indirect cost rates. If you don't have one, you can typically claim 10% de minimis rate.

Many private foundations cap indirect costs at 10-15%, or don't allow them at all. Read the guidelines carefully.

## The Budget Narrative: Your Secret Weapon

A budget narrative (or budget justification) explains *why* each line item is necessary and *how* you calculated the cost.

### What to Include

For each major category, explain:
1. What the expense is
2. How you calculated the amount
3. Why it's necessary for the project

### Example Budget Narrative

**Personnel**

*Project Director (0.5 FTE) - $35,000*
The Project Director will oversee all program activities, supervise staff, manage funder relationships, and ensure project goals are met. This position is calculated at 50% of a $70,000 annual salary, reflecting the time dedicated to this project.

*Program Coordinator (1.0 FTE) - $42,000*
The Program Coordinator will implement daily program activities, recruit and support participants, collect data, and coordinate with community partners. This full-time position is essential for serving our target of 150 participants.

**Travel**

*Local Travel - $1,800*
Staff will travel to partner sites and participant homes for outreach and support. Calculated at approximately 225 miles/month × 12 months × $0.67/mile.

## Common Budget Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

### 1. Round Numbers Everywhere
**Problem**: $50,000 for personnel, $10,000 for supplies, $5,000 for travel
**Why it's bad**: Looks like you haven't done actual calculations
**Fix**: Show your math. $47,250 is more credible than $47,000.

### 2. Forgetting Fringe Benefits
**Problem**: Only including salaries
**Why it's bad**: Your actual costs are 25-35% higher
**Fix**: Always include fringe benefits, either as a percentage or broken out

### 3. Underbudgeting to Seem Efficient
**Problem**: Cutting corners to submit a lower budget
**Why it's bad**: You won't be able to complete the project
**Fix**: Budget realistically. Funders prefer realistic budgets over artificially low ones.

### 4. No Contingency Planning
**Problem**: Every dollar accounted for with no flexibility
**Why it's bad**: Unexpected costs always arise
**Fix**: Some funders allow a contingency line (typically 5-10%). If not, build modest cushions into variable categories.

### 5. Ignoring Indirect Costs
**Problem**: Not budgeting for overhead
**Why it's bad**: Your organization loses money on every grant
**Fix**: Always include indirect costs at the maximum allowed rate

### 6. Mismatched Budget and Narrative
**Problem**: Your budget says you'll hire 2 staff, but the narrative mentions 3
**Why it's bad**: Signals poor planning or rushed writing
**Fix**: Review both documents side by side before submitting

## Matching Funds and Cost-Sharing

Many grants require matching funds—contributions from your organization or other sources.

### Types of Match

**Cash match**: Actual funds you contribute (from your budget, other grants, or donations)

**In-kind match**: Non-cash contributions like:
- Volunteer time (typically valued at $25-30/hour)
- Donated space
- Donated supplies or equipment

### Documenting Match

Keep detailed records of how you'll meet match requirements:
- Source of funds
- How the contribution is valued
- When it will be available

**Warning**: Don't promise match you can't deliver. Funders verify, and failing to meet match requirements can result in reduced funding or grant termination.

## Multi-Year Budgets

For grants spanning 2-5 years:

1. **Project inflation**: Increase personnel costs 2-3% annually
2. **Startup vs. steady-state**: Year 1 may have higher equipment/setup costs
3. **Phased activities**: Costs should align with your project timeline
4. **Sustainability**: Show how costs decrease or shift to other funding sources

## Federal Grant Budgets: Special Considerations

Federal grants have specific requirements:

### SF-424A Form
Most federal grants require this standardized budget form. It has fixed categories—map your budget to their structure.

### Cost Principles
Federal funds must follow 2 CFR 200 (Uniform Guidance):
- Costs must be reasonable, allowable, and allocable
- Some costs are explicitly unallowable (alcohol, lobbying, entertainment)
- Prior approval required for some expenses

### Indirect Cost Rate Agreement
If you receive significant federal funding, consider negotiating an indirect cost rate with your cognizant agency. This can recover significantly more overhead than the 10% de minimis rate.

## Budget Review Checklist

Before submitting, verify:

- [ ] Math is correct (totals match)
- [ ] Budget aligns with narrative
- [ ] All required categories included
- [ ] Costs are realistic and justifiable
- [ ] Match requirements are met
- [ ] Indirect costs included at correct rate
- [ ] No unallowable costs
- [ ] Follows funder's format requirements
