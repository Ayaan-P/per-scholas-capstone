Your nonprofit has found the perfect funding opportunity. The foundation's mission aligns with your work, the grant amount matches your needs, and the deadline is manageable. There's just one catch: they require a letter of inquiry before you can submit a full proposal.

That two-page document might seem like a minor hurdle, but it's actually one of the most important pieces of writing your organization will produce. A well-crafted letter of inquiry (LOI) opens doors to funding conversations. A mediocre one closes them before they ever begin.

This comprehensive guide will walk you through everything you need to know about writing letters of inquiry that get results—from understanding what foundations are really looking for to crafting each section with precision.

## What Is a Letter of Inquiry and Why Does It Matter?

A letter of inquiry—sometimes called a letter of intent or letter of interest—is a brief document (typically 1-3 pages) that introduces your organization and proposed project to a funder. Think of it as your organization's elevator pitch in written form.

Foundations use LOIs as a screening mechanism. Rather than reviewing hundreds of full proposals, they ask grant seekers to submit a condensed version first. This serves multiple purposes:

**For funders:**
- Saves time by filtering out misaligned applicants early
- Allows program officers to quickly assess fit with their priorities
- Reduces the administrative burden of processing full proposals

**For your nonprofit:**
- Saves you from writing a complete proposal for an unlikely match
- Provides early feedback on whether your project interests the funder
- Builds an initial relationship before the full application process

Here's what many grant writers don't realize: approximately 70% of LOIs never advance to the full proposal stage. This isn't because most nonprofits do poor work—it's because most LOIs fail to clearly demonstrate alignment with funder priorities.

The good news? Understanding what makes an LOI effective dramatically increases your chances of moving forward.

## Letter of Inquiry vs. Letter of Intent: Understanding the Terminology

Before diving into the writing process, let's clear up common terminology confusion:

**Letter of Inquiry (LOI):** A brief proposal summary sent to gauge funder interest. This is what you submit when a foundation asks you to inquire about potential funding before submitting a full application.

**Letter of Intent:** Sometimes used interchangeably with letter of inquiry, but can also mean a formal declaration that you plan to submit a full proposal by a certain deadline. Some government grants require letters of intent before the proposal deadline.

**Letter of Interest:** Another term used interchangeably with letter of inquiry. The meaning depends on the specific funder's guidelines.

The key takeaway: Always read the funder's specific instructions carefully. They'll define exactly what they want, regardless of the terminology they use.

## When Do Foundations Require Letters of Inquiry?

Not all funders require LOIs. Here's when you'll typically encounter them:

**Private foundations:** Many family foundations and larger private foundations use LOIs as their primary intake method. The Conrad N. Hilton Foundation, Kresge Foundation, and Robert Wood Johnson Foundation all use LOI processes.

**Community foundations:** Local and regional community foundations often prefer LOIs to manage the high volume of applications from their service areas.

**Corporate giving programs:** Companies with formal giving programs frequently use LOIs to screen applicants before inviting full proposals.

**Capacity-building funders:** Foundations focused on organizational development often use LOIs to identify nonprofits that might be good fits for their support.

Some funders make decisions based solely on the LOI—you may never write a full proposal. Others use the LOI purely for screening before inviting selected organizations to apply. Understanding which situation you're in helps you calibrate how much detail to include.

## The 7 Essential Components of an Effective Letter of Inquiry

Every successful LOI contains these core elements, presented in a logical flow:

### 1. The Opening Hook (1 paragraph)

Your first paragraph must accomplish three things:
- Name your organization
- State the amount you're requesting (if applicable)
- Capture attention with a compelling reason why this funder should care

**Weak opening:**
"Dear Program Officer, We are writing to inquire about funding opportunities at your foundation. Our organization does important work in the community."

**Strong opening:**
"Last year, 847 families in Hamilton County lost their homes to foreclosure. The Hamilton Housing Coalition has prevented 312 additional foreclosures through our emergency intervention program—but our waiting list has grown to 400 families. We are seeking a $75,000 grant from the Community Foundation to expand our housing counselor team and eliminate that waitlist within 18 months."

Notice how the strong opening leads with the problem (hook), demonstrates credibility (what you've accomplished), and clearly states what you need. The funder immediately understands the situation and your solution.

### 2. Statement of Need (1-2 paragraphs)

This section answers: Why does this problem matter, and why does it matter now?

Effective need statements include:
- Specific, current data about the problem
- Geographic and demographic scope
- Why existing solutions are insufficient
- Urgency factors (why action is needed now)

**Best practices for need statements:**

Use local data when possible. National statistics matter less than what's happening in your community. A funder in Portland cares more about Portland homelessness rates than national averages.

Humanize the numbers. After presenting statistics, include a brief example or quote that puts a human face on the issue. "These aren't just numbers—Maria, a single mother of three, came to us after losing her job and facing eviction within 30 days."

Avoid guilt-tripping. Present the need objectively. Funders are turned off by emotional manipulation or apocalyptic language.

Show the gap. What's not being done that should be? Where are current efforts falling short? This positions your organization as filling a genuine gap rather than duplicating existing work.

### 3. Your Proposed Solution (2-3 paragraphs)

This is where you describe what you'll actually do with the funding. Be specific about:

- Program activities and services
- Timeline and milestones
- Target population and geographic area
- Number of people you'll serve
- Key staff involved

Structure this section around outcomes, not activities. Don't just list what you'll do—explain what will change as a result.

**Activity-focused (weak):**
"We will hire two additional housing counselors, conduct outreach in low-income neighborhoods, and provide one-on-one counseling sessions."

**Outcome-focused (strong):**
"With two additional housing counselors, we will serve 400 families currently on our waitlist, preventing an estimated 200 foreclosures over 18 months. Our counselors will provide one-on-one financial coaching, mortgage negotiation support, and connection to emergency assistance programs. Participants will leave with a personalized housing stability plan and ongoing support through our case management system."

### 4. Organizational Background (1 paragraph)

Briefly establish your credibility. Include:
- When your organization was founded
- Core mission (one sentence)
- Key accomplishments relevant to this proposal
- Geographic service area
- Annual budget and staff size (brief mention)

Resist the urge to include your entire organizational history. Focus only on what demonstrates your capacity to execute this specific project.

**Example:**
"Founded in 2008, the Hamilton Housing Coalition has served over 5,000 families facing housing instability. Our three-person team leverages partnerships with 12 local banks and the county housing authority to negotiate solutions that keep families in their homes. Last year, 89% of our clients maintained stable housing 12 months after completing our program. We operate on an annual budget of $380,000, with diversified funding from government contracts, foundation grants, and individual donors."

### 5. Budget Summary (1 paragraph or brief table)

For an LOI, you typically don't need a full line-item budget. Provide:
- Total project cost
- Amount requested from this funder
- Other funding sources (confirmed and pending)
- What the grant will specifically cover

If the project is entirely funded by this request, say so. If you're seeking partial funding, explain how the full project will be funded.

**Example format:**
"The total project cost is $150,000 over 18 months. We are requesting $75,000 from the Community Foundation to cover salary and benefits for one full-time housing counselor. The remaining $75,000 will come from our existing HUD contract ($50,000, confirmed) and a pending request to the Local Family Foundation ($25,000, decision expected June 2026)."

### 6. Evaluation Approach (1 paragraph)

Briefly describe how you'll measure success. Include:
- Key metrics you'll track
- Data collection methods
- How you'll know the project worked

You don't need a full evaluation plan—just demonstrate that you've thought about accountability.

**Example:**
"We will track housing outcomes for all participants at 3, 6, and 12 months post-intervention. Success metrics include: foreclosures prevented, participants maintaining stable housing, and average credit score improvement. We use Salesforce to track client progress and will provide quarterly reports to all funders."

### 7. The Closing (1 paragraph)

End with:
- A brief restatement of your request
- Appreciation for the funder's consideration
- Your contact information
- An invitation to discuss further or visit your program

**Example:**
"A $75,000 investment from the Community Foundation will enable the Hamilton Housing Coalition to prevent 200 foreclosures and help 400 families achieve housing stability. We would welcome the opportunity to discuss this proposal further or host a site visit to introduce you to our work. Thank you for considering this request. Please contact me at director@hamiltonhousing.org or (555) 123-4567 with any questions."

## How Long Should a Letter of Inquiry Be?

The standard range is 1-3 pages, with most LOIs landing at 1.5-2 pages. Always check the funder's guidelines for specific requirements.

If a funder says "maximum two pages," they mean it. Exceeding page limits signals that you can't follow directions—not a good look when asking for money.

When no length is specified, aim for two pages. This provides enough space to make your case without overwhelming the reader.

## Letter of Inquiry Format and Structure

### Professional Formatting Standards

- Use standard business letter format
- 11 or 12-point font (Times New Roman, Arial, or Calibri)
- 1-inch margins
- Single-spaced with blank lines between paragraphs
- Include your organization's letterhead
- Date the letter
- Address to a specific person when possible

### Recommended Structure

**Header:** Your organization's letterhead with logo

**Date and recipient information:**
- Date
- Recipient's name and title
- Foundation name
- Address

**Salutation:** "Dear Ms. Chen," (never "To Whom It May Concern")

**Body:** Your 7 components, organized into clear paragraphs

**Closing:** "Sincerely," followed by your signature, printed name, and title

**Attachments (only if requested):**
- Annual budget
- Board list
- 501(c)(3) determination letter
- Recent annual report

## 10 Common Letter of Inquiry Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Generic, Non-Customized Letters

The biggest LOI mistake is sending the same letter to every funder. Program officers can spot a form letter immediately. Each LOI should reference the specific foundation's priorities and explain why your work aligns with their particular interests.

### 2. Burying the Ask

Don't make readers hunt for what you want. State your request clearly in the first paragraph. "We are seeking a $50,000 grant to..." is clear and direct.

### 3. Too Much Organizational History

Funders don't need to know about your founding story in detail, every program you've ever run, or awards from a decade ago. Focus on current, relevant qualifications.

### 4. Vague Problem Statements

"Many people in our community struggle with food insecurity" is too general. How many? Where exactly? What's causing it? Use specific, local data.

### 5. Activity Lists Without Outcomes

Describing activities without connecting them to results fails to show impact. Every activity should link to a measurable change.

### 6. Ignoring Funder Guidelines

If a foundation says "Do not include attachments," don't include attachments. If they request a specific format, use it exactly. Read guidelines twice before writing.

### 7. Jargon and Acronyms

Don't assume funders know your field's terminology. Spell out acronyms on first use. Replace jargon with plain language.

### 8. Weak or Missing Budget Information

Even a brief LOI needs financial clarity. How much do you need? For what, specifically? What's the total project cost?

### 9. No Demonstration of Organizational Capacity

Funders need to believe you can actually execute the project. Include evidence: staff qualifications, past successes, partnerships, infrastructure.

### 10. Typos and Errors

Nothing undermines credibility faster than sloppy writing. Proofread multiple times. Have someone unfamiliar with the project read for clarity.

- Purchase three fuel-efficient delivery vans to replace vehicles with 200,000+ miles
- Expand daily routes to serve 50 additional homebound seniors
- Reduce vehicle maintenance costs by an estimated $12,000 annually
- Ensure reliable service through harsh Illinois winters

Our meal delivery program maintains a 98% reliability rate, delivering over 45,000 meals last year with a team of 3 staff and 25 volunteers. We are funded through Area Agency on Aging contracts, foundation grants, and individual donations, with an annual budget of $320,000.

The total vehicle replacement cost is $52,000. We have secured $12,000 from our operating reserves, and we are requesting $40,000 from the Community Investment Foundation. These vehicles will serve our program for approximately 8 years, benefiting over 400 seniors during that time.

We track client health outcomes in partnership with the county health department, measuring emergency room visits, medication adherence, and self-reported wellbeing among our clients. Ninety-two percent of current clients report improved quality of life since joining the program.

Thank you for considering this request. A reliable vehicle fleet is the foundation of our service—we cannot deliver meals without the ability to reach our clients. I would welcome the opportunity to discuss this proposal further or to arrange a visit so you can meet the seniors we serve. Please contact me at director@madisonmeals.org or (555) 234-5678.

James Rodriguez
Executive Director
Madison Meals Program

## How to Research Funders Before Writing Your LOI

The most effective LOIs demonstrate clear alignment with funder priorities. This requires research before you write.

### Research Steps

**1. Review the foundation's website thoroughly**
- Mission and values statements
- Current program areas and priorities
- Recent grants made (amounts, recipients, project types)
- Any stated preferences or restrictions

**2. Read their 990 tax returns**
- Available on Candid's Foundation Directory or GuideStar
- Shows actual grants made, board members, assets
- Reveals giving patterns and priorities in practice

**3. Look for funder communications**
- Annual reports
- Blog posts or newsletters
- Published interviews with program officers
- Social media presence

**4. Research their recent grantees**
- What do successful applicants have in common?
- What's the typical grant size?
- What types of projects do they fund?

**5. Check for personal connections**
- Do any board members have ties to your organization?
- Has anyone on your team worked with this funder before?
- Can you request an informational call with a program officer?

## Following Up After Submitting Your Letter of Inquiry

After submitting your LOI, patience is required. Most foundations provide timeline expectations—respect them.

### Typical Timeline

- **Acknowledgment:** Many funders confirm receipt within 1-2 weeks
- **Initial review:** 4-8 weeks for most foundations
- **Decision communication:** You'll receive either a decline or an invitation to submit a full proposal

### Appropriate Follow-Up

**If you haven't heard back within the stated timeframe:** One polite email inquiry is appropriate. "I wanted to confirm you received our letter of inquiry submitted on [date] and ask about your expected timeline for review."

**If you receive an invitation to submit a full proposal:** Respond promptly with enthusiasm. Ask any clarifying questions about the proposal requirements.

**If you receive a decline:** Thank them graciously. Ask if they would be willing to share brief feedback. Some funders will tell you why your request wasn't a fit, which helps future applications.

## Key Takeaways for Writing Effective Letters of Inquiry

1. **Customize every LOI** to the specific funder's priorities and interests
2. **Lead with impact**—state what you'll accomplish, not just what you'll do
3. **Be concise**—respect the funder's time with tight, purposeful writing
4. **Include specific data** about the problem and your proposed solution
5. **Demonstrate organizational capacity** to execute successfully
6. **Follow guidelines exactly**—page limits, required components, attachments
7. **Proofread meticulously**—errors signal carelessness
8. **Research thoroughly** before writing to ensure genuine alignment

A strong letter of inquiry doesn't guarantee funding, but it dramatically increases your chances of getting to the next stage. Put in the work upfront to craft a compelling LOI, and you'll open more doors to the funding your mission deserves.
