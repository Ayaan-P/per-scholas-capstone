# How to Find Foundation Grants for Nonprofits: The Complete Guide to Private Funding

While government grants get most of the attention in nonprofit circles, **private foundation grants represent over $90 billion in annual giving** — and they're often easier to secure than federal funding. Foundation grants typically have simpler applications, faster decisions, and more flexible use of funds.

Yet many nonprofits overlook foundation funding because they don't know where to look. This guide will show you exactly how to find, research, and win foundation grants for your organization.

## Understanding Foundation Types

Before diving into grant research, it's important to understand the different types of foundations and how they operate.

### Private Foundations

Private foundations are funded by an individual, family, or corporation. They're required by law to distribute approximately 5% of their assets annually as grants. Examples include:

- **The Ford Foundation** — $16 billion in assets, focuses on inequality and justice
- **Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation** — The largest private foundation globally
- **The Kresge Foundation** — Focuses on expanding opportunity in cities
- **MacArthur Foundation** — Known for "genius grants" and criminal justice reform

Private foundations often have dedicated program officers and clear funding priorities published on their websites.

### Family Foundations

A subset of private foundations, family foundations are controlled by family members of the original donor. They represent about 50% of all private foundations in the US. Family foundations:

- Often have smaller staff (sometimes none)
- May have less formal application processes
- Frequently fund in specific geographic areas
- Can be more relationship-driven in their grantmaking

### Corporate Foundations

Corporate foundations are established by businesses but operate as separate legal entities. They typically fund causes aligned with the company's industry or values:

- **Walmart Foundation** — Community development and workforce programs
- **Google.org** — Technology for social impact
- **Bank of America Charitable Foundation** — Economic mobility and environmental sustainability

Corporate foundations often prefer grants that align with their brand values and sometimes prioritize communities where they operate.

### Community Foundations

Community foundations pool donations from many sources to serve a specific geographic area. Examples include:

- The Chicago Community Foundation
- Silicon Valley Community Foundation
- The Cleveland Foundation

These are excellent sources for **local nonprofits** and often have streamlined application processes.

## Where to Search for Foundation Grants

### Foundation Directory Online (FDO)

The gold standard for foundation research, [Foundation Directory Online](https://fconline.foundationcenter.org/) by Candid offers the most comprehensive database of US foundations. While it requires a subscription, many public libraries offer free access.

With FDO, you can:
- Search 140,000+ US foundations
- Filter by giving areas, geographic focus, and grant size
- View past grants made by each foundation
- Access foundation contact information and key staff

**Pro tip:** Look up foundations that have funded organizations similar to yours. Their past giving is the best predictor of future grants.

### IRS Form 990s

Every foundation must file an annual Form 990-PF with the IRS, which becomes public information. These forms reveal:

- Total grants awarded
- List of all grant recipients with amounts
- Foundation assets and giving capacity
- Names of trustees and officers

You can access 990s for free through [Candid's 990 Finder](https://candid.org/research-and-verify-nonprofits/990-finder) or [ProPublica's Nonprofit Explorer](https://projects.propublica.org/nonprofits/).

### Foundation Websites

Once you've identified potential funders, visit their websites directly. Look for:

- **Funding priorities** — Most foundations list specific program areas
- **Eligibility requirements** — Geographic restrictions, organizational budget limits, etc.
- **Application guidelines** — Many foundations only accept proposals during certain windows
- **Past grantees** — Published grant lists help you gauge fit

### State Association of Nonprofits

Your state's nonprofit association often maintains databases of local funders. These resources typically focus on foundations with regional giving priorities — exactly the funders most likely to support your work.

### Nonprofit Journals and News

Publications like *The Chronicle of Philanthropy*, *Inside Philanthropy*, and *Philanthropy News Digest* regularly report on foundation giving trends and new funding initiatives. Following these sources helps you spot opportunities early.

## Researching Foundation Fit

Finding foundations is only the first step. The real skill is identifying which foundations are **genuinely likely to fund your work**. Here's how to assess fit:

### Analyze Past Grants

The best predictor of who a foundation will fund is who they've funded before. When reviewing a foundation's giving history, ask:

1. **What's the typical grant size?** Don't pursue $500K grants if you need $10K.
2. **What types of organizations receive funding?** Direct service providers? Advocacy groups? Research institutions?
3. **What's the geographic pattern?** National funders vs. local/regional focus?
4. **How specific are their program interests?** Broad "education" funding vs. "after-school STEM for low-income middle schoolers"?

### Check Application Requirements

Before investing time in an application, verify you meet basic requirements:

- **501(c)(3) status** — Most foundations require this
- **Organizational budget** — Some funders have minimum or maximum budget thresholds
- **Operating history** — New organizations may be ineligible for some grants
- **Geographic location** — Many foundations restrict funding to specific regions

### Understand Foundation Culture

Every foundation has its own culture and values. Research suggests looking at:

- **Board composition** — Who are the trustees and what do they care about?
- **Staff backgrounds** — Former practitioners vs. career philanthropists?
- **Communication style** — Formal and structured vs. conversational?
- **Theory of change** — Do they fund proven programs or innovation?

## Building Relationships with Foundations

Foundation giving is inherently relational. Unlike government grants (which are purely competitive), foundation grants often go to organizations the foundation knows and trusts.

### Before You Apply

1. **Attend funder briefings** — Many foundations host informational sessions about their priorities
2. **Meet at conferences** — Foundation staff often attend sector conferences
3. **Connect through your network** — Ask board members or partners for introductions
4. **Follow on social media** — Engage thoughtfully with foundation content

### During the Application Process

1. **Ask clarifying questions** — Most program officers appreciate thoughtful inquiries
2. **Request a pre-submission conversation** — Some foundations offer preliminary feedback
3. **Be honest about challenges** — Funders respect transparency
4. **Show your work** — Document your logic model and evidence base

### After a Grant (or Rejection)

1. **Report thoroughly and on time** — This is critical for future funding
2. **Stay in touch** — Share newsworthy updates even when not required
3. **Invite site visits** — Let funders see your work firsthand
4. **Ask for feedback on rejections** — Learn why and try again if appropriate

## Writing Winning Foundation Proposals

Foundation proposals differ from government applications in important ways:

### Keep It Concise

Most foundation proposals are 5-10 pages, not 50. Funders read hundreds of proposals — respect their time with clear, direct writing.

### Lead with Need

Start by establishing the problem you're solving. Use local data and human stories to make the need vivid and specific. Avoid generic statistics that could apply anywhere.

### Show, Don't Tell

Instead of claiming you're "effective" or "innovative," demonstrate it:
- Share outcome data from past programs
- Include testimonials from beneficiaries
- Describe what makes your approach unique
- Acknowledge what you're still learning

### Budget Realistically

Foundation program officers can spot inflated budgets. Present costs that are:
- Reasonable for your region and sector
- Clearly connected to program activities
- Detailed enough to show planning
- Honest about indirect costs (most foundations allow them)

### Address Sustainability

Foundations want to know their investment will have lasting impact. Explain:
- How the program will continue after the grant
- Other funding sources you're pursuing
- How learnings will be shared with the field

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Spraying and Praying

Mass-applying to every foundation wastes everyone's time. Focus your energy on funders who are genuinely aligned with your mission and approach.

### Ignoring Guidelines

If a foundation says "no unsolicited proposals," believe them. If they ask for a two-page letter of inquiry, don't send a 20-page proposal. Following instructions demonstrates organizational competence.

### Overselling Outcomes

Promising unrealistic results damages credibility. Be ambitious but honest about what's achievable with the proposed funding.

### Forgetting the Follow-Up

Many organizations neglect to report properly after receiving grants. This burns the relationship and closes the door to future funding.

### Not Tracking Your Outreach

Use a CRM or spreadsheet to track:
- Foundations you've researched
- Application deadlines
- Submitted proposals
- Outcomes and feedback
- Follow-up actions needed

## Building a Foundation Funding Strategy

Sustainable foundation funding isn't about chasing one-off grants. It's about building a **portfolio of funders** who believe in your work.

### Diversify Your Funders

Avoid depending too heavily on any single foundation. A healthy funding mix might include:

- 2-3 larger multi-year grants from national foundations
- Several medium-sized grants from regional funders
- A base of smaller grants from family and community foundations
- Government contracts where appropriate
- Individual donor support

### Think Multi-Year

The most valuable foundation relationships are multi-year partnerships. Many foundations offer multi-year grants, but even those that don't often fund the same organizations repeatedly. Focus on becoming a trusted grantee rather than seeking one-time windfalls.

### Align Your Board

Board members can be invaluable in foundation fundraising through:
- Personal connections to foundation trustees
- Attendance at funder events
- Strategic advice on positioning
- In-kind expertise in grant writing

### Document Everything

Keep records of:
- Foundation research and notes
- All correspondence with program officers
- Proposal submissions and outcomes
- Reporting requirements and deadlines
- Relationships and contact history
